Tomás's Story

Tomás had been reading gas meters across Galway for eleven years. He knew the job inside out — the routes, the houses, the timing. It was steady work, and he took pride in doing it safely and professionally. On a Tuesday afternoon in May, he knocked on a door in a quiet residential estate, as he had done thousands of times before.

When the owner answered, they seemed friendly enough. Tomás explained he was there to read the meter, and the homeowner said he'd meet him inside. What happened next changed everything. As Tomás stepped through the hallway, an uncontrolled dog emerged from a back room and bit him on the leg and arm. The injuries were immediate — bleeding, pain, and shock. Tomás called for help, and an ambulance took him to the hospital where he needed twelve stitches and a course of antibiotics to prevent infection.

The worst part came next. When Tomás reported the incident to his employer, the company told him it was his own fault. They said he should have seen warning signs, that he should have asked about dogs in the house. His managers suggested he'd been careless. Nothing was documented. Nothing was recorded. And when Tomás needed time off to recover from the wound and the shock of the attack, he wasn't paid. Four weeks of lost wages added up quickly.

Tomás knew this wasn't right. He hadn't been negligent. He'd done exactly what he always did. But he didn't know where to turn. He was sore, he was angry, and he was broke because of something that wasn't his fault. That's when he contacted eSolicitors.ie to understand what his rights actually were.

Within weeks, Tomás had his answer in writing: the law was on his side. The homeowner was liable for the bite, regardless of whether the dog had bitten anyone before. Tomás didn't have to prove anything about the dog's history. And his lost wages — every penny of them — were part of what he was entitled to claim. A settlement was negotiated that covered his medical costs, his pain and suffering, and four weeks of lost earnings. Justice, at last.

What the Law Says

If you're bitten by a dog in Ireland, the law is clear and it's in your favour. The key law here is the Control of Dogs Act 1986.

Here's what you need to know:

  • The owner is liable. It doesn't matter if the dog has never bitten anyone before. It doesn't matter if the owner says the dog is friendly or has never shown aggression. Once a bite happens, the owner is responsible — this is called "strict liability".
  • You don't have to prove negligence. You don't have to show that the owner was careless or knew the dog was dangerous. The bite itself is enough.
  • The owner should have controlled their dog. Dogs in Ireland should be under control at all times on public or private property. If they're not, and they bite you, the owner is liable.
  • It applies everywhere. Your own home, someone else's home, a workplace, a public space — it doesn't matter. If you're bitten, the owner is liable.

This is one of the strongest areas of Irish law when it comes to personal injury. The law assumes the owner's responsibility, not yours.

What Compensation Looks Like

When you're bitten by a dog, you can claim compensation for several things. Here's what that typically includes:

Medical Expenses

All costs related to treating your bite wound — hospital visits, stitches, antibiotics, tetanus shots, and any follow-up care. Keep every receipt and medical record.

Scarring and Permanent Injury

Dog bites often leave scars, especially on the face, hands, or neck. Compensation reflects the visibility and impact of these scars on your daily life and appearance.

Pain and Suffering

The physical pain of the injury and the psychological trauma — anxiety around dogs, nightmares, or fear of going outside — all count toward your claim.

Lost Income

If you missed work while recovering, you can claim for those lost wages. If the injury affects your ability to work long-term, that's factored in too.

Future Care Costs

For serious bites, you might need ongoing physiotherapy, counselling, or cosmetic surgery. These future costs are included in your settlement.

The amount you receive depends on the severity of the bite, the evidence you gather, and how well your solicitor argues your case.

  • He took photos of his wound and the scar as it healed.

How to Make Your Dog Bite Claim

If you've been bitten by a dog in Ireland, follow these steps:

  1. Get Medical Help — Even if the bite seems minor, see a doctor or go to A&E. Dog bites can get infected quickly, and you need medical records for your claim.
  2. Document Everything — Take photos of the bite, the wound, and any scarring as it develops. Write down what happened: the date, time, location, the dog's appearance, and the owner's details.
  3. Report to Gardaí — File a report with local gardaí. This creates an official record and can help if there are other complaints about the same dog.
  4. Get Witness Details — If anyone saw the attack, get their names and contact information. Their statement can strengthen your claim.
  5. Find a Solicitor — Look for a solicitor who specialises in personal injury or dog bite claims. Many offer free initial consultations.
  6. Gather Evidence — Provide your solicitor with medical records, photos, receipts for treatment, proof of lost wages, and any correspondence with the dog owner or their insurance company.
  7. Submit Your Claim — Your solicitor will contact the dog owner's home insurance company with a formal claim letter and supporting evidence.
  8. Negotiate or Go to Court — Most claims settle out of court. If the insurance company disputes your claim, your solicitor can take it to the District Court or Circuit Court.

What Can Go Wrong (And How to Avoid It)

Not every dog bite claim is straightforward. Here are common obstacles and how to handle them:

The Owner Claims You Provoked the Dog

This is a common defence. The law still holds the owner liable, but if you can show you weren't provoking the dog, it strengthens your case. Witness statements help here.

You Didn't Report It to Gardaí

This doesn't kill your claim, but it weakens it. Always report to gardaí, even if the injury seems minor. It creates an official record.

The Owner Has No Insurance

If the owner is uninsured, you can still pursue a claim directly against them. You can go to court and pursue a judgment, though collecting money might be difficult. This is where legal aid or a "no win, no fee" agreement with your solicitor becomes valuable.

You Delayed Getting Medical Help

If you wait weeks to see a doctor, the insurance company may argue the injury wasn't serious or question whether the dog actually caused it. Get medical help immediately.

You Don't Have Proof of the Owner's Identity

If you didn't get the owner's details at the scene, ask gardaí to help identify them. Gardaí can use CCTV from nearby businesses or canvass the area to find the owner.

How Much Will It Cost You?

This is one of the best parts of making a dog bite claim in Ireland: it often costs you nothing upfront.

No Win, No Fee

Most personal injury solicitors in Ireland work on a "no win, no fee" basis. This means you don't pay them anything unless they win your claim. When you do win, they take a percentage of your compensation (typically 25–33%). You'll also pay case costs (things like court fees, medical reports, and expert witnesses), but only if you win.

Legal Aid

If you're on a low income, you might qualify for legal aid from the State. This covers solicitor and barrister fees. You'd need to apply through the Legal Aid Board.
