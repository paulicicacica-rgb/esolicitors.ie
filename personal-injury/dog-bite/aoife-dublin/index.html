Bitten by a Neighbour's Dog in Dublin: Aoife's Successful Claim

A dog slipped its lead in a communal green. The owner said it had never bitten anyone before. But the law doesn't work that way.

What Happened

Aoife was taking a shortcut through the communal green behind her apartment block in Ballymun one afternoon when she noticed a golden retriever running loose. The dog had slipped its lead, and its owner was chasing after it about thirty metres away. Before Aoife could move, the dog bounded towards her and bit her on the left arm.

The injury was serious enough to need hospital treatment. Aoife had deep puncture wounds that required stitches and left visible scarring. Beyond the physical injury, she developed anxiety about going outside, particularly around dogs. What should have been a normal walk through her own neighbourhood had left her shaken and injured.

When the owner arrived, he was apologetic and insisted the dog had never done anything like this before. He'd owned the dog for five years without incident. He assumed this meant he wasn't really liable, or that the situation was just an unfortunate accident that could have happened to anyone. He wasn't keen on involving solicitors or insurance companies.

But Aoife knew she had incurred real costs — medical bills, lost wages from time off work, and ongoing anxiety that affected her quality of life. She decided to speak to a solicitor, and that decision made all the difference. Under Irish law, it turns out that the dog's perfect track record was actually irrelevant. The owner was liable regardless.

What the Law Says

In Ireland, dog bite claims are governed by the Control of Dogs Act 1986. This law is actually very straightforward and very much in favour of the person who's been injured.

The key principle is called strict liability. This means the dog's owner is liable for any injury caused by the dog, full stop. The owner cannot escape liability by arguing that the dog has never bitten anyone before, or that it's normally well-behaved, or that the bite was completely out of character.

The only exceptions are narrow ones: if you were trespassing, or if you deliberately provoked the dog. In Aoife's case, she was using a communal green she had every right to use, and she hadn't provoked anything. She was simply walking through.

The law recognises that dog owners have a responsibility to keep their dogs under control. If a dog causes injury because the owner has failed to do that — whether through a slipped lead, a broken fence, or any other means — the owner is responsible for the consequences. The dog's past behaviour is irrelevant.

What Aoife Was Entitled To

Once a solicitor took up her case, the insurance company (which the dog owner did have) settled relatively quickly. Aoife received compensation that covered several things:

Aoife's Compensation Included:

  • Medical costs: All her hospital treatment, stitches, and follow-up wound care
  • Lost earnings: The two weeks she couldn't work while she recovered from the wound
  • Scarring: Permanent scarring on her arm — this accounts for a significant portion of compensation because it's permanent and visible
  • Psychological impact: The anxiety she developed about going outside and being near dogs
  • General damages: Compensation for pain, suffering, and loss of amenity

In total, Aoife received €22,500. This falls in the moderate to upper-moderate range for dog bite injuries in Ireland. If her injuries had been more severe — for instance, if she'd needed surgery, or if the scarring had been much more extensive, or if the psychological impact had been more severe — the compensation could easily have been higher.

Compensation Ranges for Dog Bite Claims

Dog bite compensation in Ireland typically depends on how serious the injury is:

Typical Compensation Ranges:

  • Minor injuries (small wound, no scarring): €2,000 to €8,000
  • Moderate injuries (stitches, some scarring, short recovery): €10,000 to €25,000
  • Serious injuries (significant scarring, loss of function, psychological impact): €25,000 to €50,000+
  • Very serious injuries (disfigurement, permanent nerve damage, severe psychological trauma): €50,000 and above

The exact amount always depends on your specific circumstances: the size and depth of the wound, whether there's scarring, how long recovery took, whether there's ongoing psychological impact, and how much your injury has affected your life. A solicitor can give you a much better estimate once they've reviewed your case in detail.

⚠️ Important: Time Limits Apply

You have 2 years from the date of the bite to make a claim for compensation. After that, you lose your right to claim. If you've been bitten by a dog, don't delay. Even if the owner seems friendly or apologetic, contact a solicitor to protect your rights. Many cases are settled much more quickly than you might expect.
