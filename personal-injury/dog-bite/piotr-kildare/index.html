Dog Bite Claim in Rural Kildare: Delivery Driver Gets Full Settlement

How Piotr won his case despite the owner having no home insurance

What Happened to Piotr

Piotr had been working as a delivery driver for just over a year when the incident happened on a Tuesday afternoon in March. He was dropping off a parcel at a rural property outside Maynooth in Kildare. The house sat back from the road, with a gravel drive leading up to the front door. As he walked towards the house with the package in his hands, a large German Shepherd came tearing around the corner of the garden fence. The dog had been left unattended in the back garden — no lead, no kennel, just loose.

Before Piotr could even knock on the door or call out, the dog had lunged and bitten his left leg, just above the ankle. He fell to the ground, and the dog bit him again on his arm as he tried to shield himself. By the time the homeowner came running out and called the dog off, Piotr was badly shaken and bleeding. He was taken by ambulance to Naas Hospital, where he received stitches and antibiotics. The wound became infected, which meant a second visit to hospital and a course of strong antibiotics.

What made things complicated was that the homeowner — a man in his sixties — didn't have home insurance. When Piotr or his family asked about making a claim, the homeowner seemed reluctant. He said he couldn't afford to pay compensation and wasn't sure what he could do. Piotr was stuck with medical bills, time off work, and psychological trauma from the attack. He was nervous about making deliveries on rural properties for months afterward.

Piotr's mother encouraged him to speak to a solicitor. That's when he learned something that changed everything: the fact that the homeowner had no insurance didn't matter. Under Irish law, the dog's owner is strictly liable for injuries caused by the dog. Piotr had a right to claim compensation, and the law was on his side.

What the Law Says About Dog Bites in Ireland

The law that protects people like Piotr is the Control of Dogs Act 1986. This law is simple and powerful: if a dog injures someone, the owner is liable. Full stop. It doesn't matter whether the dog has bitten anyone before. It doesn't matter if the owner was careless or reckless. It doesn't even matter if the dog is usually friendly.

This is called strict liability, and it means the owner cannot escape responsibility by saying "my dog has never done this before" or "I didn't know the dog would attack." The law holds the owner responsible for keeping their dog under control. If the dog injures someone, the owner pays for it.

In Piotr's case, the homeowner had left the dog unattended in an unfenced area where members of the public — like delivery drivers — regularly came to the front door. This made the situation even clearer. The dog was not under control, and the owner should have known it posed a risk.

There are three main situations where a dog owner is liable under Irish law: the dog causes injury to a person, the dog causes damage to livestock or other animals, or the dog causes damage to property. Piotr's case fell into the first category.

What Compensation Looks Like

Compensation for a dog bite isn't a lottery. The courts follow clear guidelines based on the severity of the injury, the long-term effects, and the impact on your life.

Typical Ranges

For moderate dog bite injuries — wounds that require stitches, cause some scarring, and take a few weeks to heal — compensation typically ranges from €10,000 to €40,000. This covers the medical costs, lost wages while you're recovering, pain and suffering, and any permanent scarring.

In Piotr's case, he was awarded €22,500. This reflected:

  • Medical costs: Hospital visits, stitches, antibiotics, and the second admission for infection.
  • Lost wages: The three weeks he was off work healing and recovering.
  • Pain and suffering: The physical pain from the bites and the scarring that will remain visible.
  • Psychological trauma: His anxiety about going to unfamiliar properties, which affected his work for several months.
  • Scarring: Two permanent scars on his leg and arm.

Serious cases involving extensive scarring, permanent disfigurement, or severe psychological trauma can be significantly higher — sometimes €50,000 or more. But Piotr's case fell into the moderate range, which is why €22,500 was awarded.
