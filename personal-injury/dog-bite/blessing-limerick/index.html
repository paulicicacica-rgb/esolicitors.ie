Knocked Down and Bitten: How Blessing Claimed Compensation for a Dog Bite Injury

A story from Limerick about recovering after a frightening attack

What Happened to Blessing

Blessing was taking her usual afternoon walk near her home in Limerick on a bright spring day. She was walking along a quiet residential street, enjoying the good weather, when she heard a loud bark. Without warning, a large dog broke free from its owner's lead and came straight at her. Everything happened in seconds. The dog jumped up and knocked her to the ground. She fell hard, the wind knocked out of her, and then the dog bit down on her calf. The pain was sharp and immediate.

The dog's owner ran over and pulled the dog away, apologising repeatedly. Blessing was shaken and bleeding. She got to her feet with help and managed to walk to a nearby café where staff called an ambulance. At the hospital, she was treated for a deep puncture wound on her lower leg, heavy bruising across her hip and ribs where she'd hit the ground, and a graze on her arm. The doctors gave her antibiotics and tetanus injections, and the wound was cleaned and dressed.

But the physical injury was only part of the story. Weeks after the attack, Blessing found herself anxious on walks. Even passing a dog at a distance made her heart race. She had nightmares about the incident and felt jumpy in everyday situations. Simple things like going to the shops became stressful if she thought she might see a dog. This psychological impact — the fear and anxiety — was as real as the bite wound, and it affected her quality of life.

Blessing contacted eSolicitors.ie to understand her rights. She learned that under Irish law, she didn't need to prove the dog owner was careless or that the dog had attacked anyone before. The law was clear: the owner was liable for the injury caused by their dog. With help, she made a claim. The compensation she received included payment for her medical treatment, the physical pain and suffering, time off work, and importantly, recognition of the psychological trauma she'd experienced.

What the Law Says

The Control of Dogs Act 1986

In Ireland, the law that protects people injured by dogs is the Control of Dogs Act 1986. This is a powerful piece of legislation because it makes the dog owner strictly liable — which means you don't have to prove they were negligent or careless.

Under this Act, if a dog injures you, the owner is responsible. It doesn't matter if:

  • The dog has never attacked anyone before
  • The owner says they were being careful
  • The dog was usually friendly
  • The owner was trying their best to control the dog

The owner's liability is strict. This is why Blessing's case was straightforward: the dog caused her injury, so the owner was liable to pay compensation.

What Blessing Was Entitled To

Compensation Typically Includes:

Medical Costs

All treatment related to the injury: ambulance, emergency room, antibiotics, dressings, follow-up appointments, and any physiotherapy or medical care needed.

Pain and Suffering

Compensation for the physical pain caused by the bite and bruises, and for the time during recovery when the injury affected daily activities.

Lost Earnings

If the injury meant time off work, compensation covers lost wages during the recovery period.

Psychological Trauma

This is an important part of many dog bite claims. The anxiety, fear, and nightmares Blessing experienced after the attack are recognised by Irish law as genuine harm. Courts award compensation for this psychological impact, especially when it affects someone's ability to go about normal life.

Scarring or Permanent Disfigurement

If the bite leaves permanent marks or scars, this is recognised in the compensation amount. The more visible the scarring, the higher the award tends to be.

Typical Compensation Ranges

For moderate dog bite injuries like Blessing's — a deep bite with bruising and psychological impact but no permanent scarring — compensation typically ranges from €10,000 to €40,000.

More serious cases involving significant scarring, permanent nerve damage, or severe ongoing psychological trauma can be awarded much higher amounts — sometimes €50,000 and above.

⏰ Important: Time Limit for Claims

You have 2 years from the date of the dog bite to make a claim.

This is a strict legal deadline. Even if you were injured badly and didn't realise you could claim, the clock started ticking from the day of the incident. After 2 years, you lose the right to claim compensation, no matter what.

If you've been bitten by a dog, don't wait. Contact a solicitor to discuss your claim as soon as possible. The sooner you act, the fresher the evidence, the clearer the memories, and the more likely you are to get fair compensation.
