Knocked Down on a Pedestrian Crossing in Galway

Grace's fight for justice after a driver ran a red light

What Happened to Grace

"It was an ordinary Thursday morning. I was crossing at the pedestrian lights on Main Street in Galway, heading to work. I'd used that same crossing a hundred times. The lights were green for pedestrians, and I was looking straight ahead. I didn't see the car coming."

Grace was hit by a silver sedan doing about 40 kilometres per hour. The driver had ignored a red light completely. She was thrown forward, landing hard on the pavement. Her left leg bent under her body, and she knew immediately something was badly wrong. People rushed to help. An ambulance arrived within ten minutes.

At Galway University Hospital, tests showed she had a severe compound fracture of her tibia and fibula — her shin bone was actually broken in two places. The doctors had to operate to insert a metal rod to hold the bones in place while they healed. She spent five days in hospital and was released with strict instructions: no weight-bearing for six weeks, then months of careful rehabilitation.

But the physical injury wasn't the worst part. Grace kept replaying the moment over and over. She had nightmares about cars, couldn't bear to cross roads, and even walking down the street made her heart race. She was diagnosed with post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD) linked to the accident. A therapist helped her understand that what she was experiencing was a real injury to her mind, not just her body.

"I was off work for four months. Even when I went back, I couldn't concentrate. I kept having panic attacks. I felt like my life had been turned upside down by someone who wasn't even looking at the road."

What the Law Says

Your Right to Be Safe

Under Irish law, drivers have a legal duty of care towards pedestrians and cyclists. This duty is set out in the Road Traffic Act and comes down to a simple principle: every driver must drive carefully and take reasonable steps to avoid injuring other people on the road.

When a driver runs a red light — as happened to Grace — they are breaking that duty. They're not driving with care; they're creating an unreasonable risk of harm. In the eyes of the law, this is called negligence.

If you're hit by a car on a pedestrian crossing when the lights are green for you, the driver is almost always legally responsible. They should have stopped. The fact that they didn't means they've failed in their legal duty.

Negligence and Liability

To have a successful claim, you need to show three things:

1. Duty of Care: The driver had a legal duty to drive safely and watch for pedestrians. ✓ Clear in Grace's case.

2. Breach: The driver broke that duty by running the red light. ✓ The driver did this.

3. Damage: You suffered harm — physical injury, psychological harm, lost income, medical costs. ✓ Grace was severely injured.

Grace's case was straightforward: the driver ran a red light, she had the right to cross, and she was seriously injured as a result. The driver's insurance company had no real defence.

What Grace Was Entitled To

When you're injured through someone else's negligence, Irish law says you should be put back in the position you would have been in if the accident hadn't happened. Because that's impossible, the law uses money. Here's what Grace's compensation covered:

Pain and Suffering (General Damages) This is the money for the injury itself — the pain during recovery, the psychological trauma, the loss of enjoyment of life. Grace's broken leg needed surgery and months of rehabilitation. The PTSD meant she couldn't do normal things like cross roads without panic. The court awarded her €42,000 for these injuries.
Lost Income (Special Damages) Grace was off work for four months. She had sick leave for the first month, but then it was unpaid. She lost €8,400 in wages. Her employer had held her job open, but she was in genuine hardship. This was fully compensated.
Medical and Treatment Costs Hospital stay, surgery, physiotherapy, psychology sessions, medications. Some of this was covered by the public health system, but Grace paid €3,200 out of pocket for private physiotherapy to speed her recovery. All of this was covered.
Travel and Care Costs Grace's mother had to take unpaid time off work to help her. She couldn't drive for three months. Taxis to hospital appointments added up. The settlement included €2,100 to cover these costs.

Grace's total settlement: €55,700

This was agreed without going to court. The driver's insurance company assessed the claim and, because the liability was clear and the injuries were genuine and well-documented, they settled. It took seven months from accident to settlement.

⏰ Important: Time Limit for Your Claim

You have 2 years from the date of the accident to take a legal claim in Ireland. This is a strict deadline. If you don't start your claim by then, you lose your right to compensation forever — no exceptions.

This doesn't mean you need to go to court within two years. It means you need to notify the other person's insurance company and begin the legal process. But don't wait. Evidence fades, memories blur, and witnesses become harder to reach. If you've been knocked down or hit by a car, contact a solicitor as soon as you can.
