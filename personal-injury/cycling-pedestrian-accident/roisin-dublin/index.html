Knocked Off My Bike by a Car Door – And How I Got Compensation

Roisin's story from Dublin

A broken collarbone, two months off work, and a settlement that helped her recover

What Happened

It was a Tuesday morning in May. I was cycling to work along a quiet residential street near Ballymun in Dublin – the same route I'd taken for five years without incident. I was going at a steady pace, keeping to the side of the road as cyclists do. The street wasn't particularly busy, and I felt safe enough.

As I approached a parked car, the driver's door suddenly swung open without any warning. There was no indicator that someone was about to get out. I had no time to brake or swerve. My front wheel caught the door, and I went flying over the handlebars. I remember hitting the ground hard on my left side and then nothing until I was lying on the road with concerned strangers around me and an ambulance on the way.

At the hospital, I was diagnosed with a fractured collarbone. It sounds like a small thing when you say it out loud, but it wasn't. I spent six weeks in a sling, unable to drive, unable to lift anything, and struggling with the most basic tasks. Two full months passed before I was well enough to return to work. My employer had been understanding, but two months without full pay put a real strain on my finances. I was frustrated, in pain, and angry that someone's carelessness had derailed my life.

What made it worse was feeling unsure about what I could actually do about it. I knew it wasn't my fault – anyone cycling past a parked car shouldn't have to expect a door to open into their path. But I didn't know if that meant I had a legal right to claim compensation, or how I'd even start that process. It felt like something I'd have to accept and move on from, even though it was clearly the driver's responsibility.

That's when I decided to reach out to a solicitor through eSolicitors.ie. Within days, I was speaking to someone who knew exactly what the law said about situations like mine, and suddenly everything became clear.

What the Law Says

My solicitor explained that under Irish law, every motorist has a duty of care to other road users – including cyclists. This isn't just good practice; it's a legal obligation set out in the Road Traffic Act. Opening a car door without checking for traffic, cyclists, or pedestrians is a breach of that duty of care.

The specific rule about car doors is straightforward: a driver or passenger must not open a door if it would endanger anyone. They must check their mirrors, look over their shoulder, and make sure it's safe. The person in the car is responsible for any injury or damage caused by a door being opened unsafely.

In my case, the driver admitted they hadn't checked. They simply opened the door without looking. Under the Road Traffic Act and common law principles of negligence, this was a clear breach of duty, and I had the right to claim compensation for the injuries and losses I suffered as a result.

It's worth noting that local authorities can also be liable in some cases if poor road or path maintenance contributed to the accident. In my situation, the road itself was fine, so liability rested entirely with the driver.

What I Was Entitled To

How Compensation Works

Compensation for a personal injury claim like mine typically covers two main areas:

Special Damages: This is the money I'd actually spent or lost. It included my medical costs (ambulance, hospital, physiotherapy), prescription medications, lost wages during the two months I couldn't work, and even the cost of replacing my damaged bike.

General Damages: This is compensation for the injury itself – the pain I experienced, the time I spent in a sling, the months of reduced quality of life, and the lasting impact on my confidence as a cyclist.

For a fractured collarbone that required six weeks of immobilisation and led to two months off work, compensation typically ranges from €15,000 to €120,000, depending on how severe the injury was and how much it affected the person's life. The range is wide because every injury is different – some people recover fully within a few months, while others experience lasting pain or complications.

In my case, I was fortunate that I recovered well without any permanent damage. The insurance company accepted full liability (which they should have – the facts were clear), and we reached a settlement of €28,500. This covered all my medical costs, my lost wages, and compensation for the pain and disruption. It wasn't going to change my life, but it did ease the financial pressure I'd been under and felt like a fair acknowledgement that what happened to me wasn't my fault.

⏰ Important: Don't Wait Too Long

You have only 2 years from the date of the accident to start a personal injury claim in Ireland.

This is a hard deadline. If you're injured in a cycling or pedestrian accident, don't put off getting advice. Even if you're not sure yet whether you'll pursue a claim, get professional advice early. Waiting too long could mean losing your right to claim entirely.

In my case, I acted within weeks, which is exactly what you should do.

The Process and Settlement

Once I'd appointed a solicitor, they handled most of the heavy lifting. They gathered evidence – my medical records, photos of the accident scene, witness statements from people who saw what happened, and documentation of my lost wages. The insurance company reviewed everything, saw that their driver was clearly at fault, and agreed to pay compensation without the need for a court case.

The whole process took about four months from first contact with my solicitor to receiving the settlement. That might sound like a long time, but for something that wasn't contested, it was actually quite straightforward. My solicitor kept me updated at each stage, and I never felt lost or unsure about what was happening.

How This Has Affected Me

Physically, I recovered well. The collarbone healed properly, and I don't have ongoing pain. Psychologically, it took longer. For months after the accident, I felt nervous cycling – I'd tense up when I saw parked cars, and I'd slow right down even on roads where there was no danger. Over time, that got better, and now I cycle normally again, though I'm definitely more aware.

The settlement gave me peace of mind in a practical sense. It meant I didn't have to worry about the medical bills I'd incurred or the lost income that had stretched me financially. It also meant that the driver and their insurance company had to acknowledge their responsibility. That mattered to me emotionally – I wasn't left feeling like this was just something I had to accept as bad luck.

What I'd Tell Someone in the Same Situation

First: Don't assume you don't have a claim. If you've been injured in a cycling or pedestrian accident because of someone else's negligence, you probably do have legal rights. Get advice.

Second: Don't delay. The two-year limit is real, and it matters. If you're injured, contact a solicitor within weeks, not months or years.

Third: Keep records. Photographs of the scene, witness names and contact details, medical reports, receipts for everything you've paid for – all of this helps your solicitor build a strong case.

Fourth: Be honest about how the accident has affected you. The compensation you receive should fairly reflect the injury and disruption you've experienced. Don't downplay it out of politeness or British reserve – this is your right.

What to Do If This Happens to You

If you've been involved in a cycling accident or knocked down as a pedestrian in Ireland, here are the key steps to take:

  1. Seek medical attention immediately – Your health is the priority. Even if injuries seem minor, get checked by a doctor and keep all medical records.
  2. Report the incident to gardaí – If the other party was at fault, file a formal report. Get the incident number for your records.
  3. Gather evidence – Take photos of the scene, your injuries, and any damage. Get contact details from witnesses.
  4. Document everything – Keep records of medical appointments, prescriptions, and any time off work.
  5. Contact a personal injury solicitor – An experienced solicitor can advise you on your claim and handle negotiations with the other party's insurance company.
  6. Don't accept the first offer – Insurance companies may offer less than you're entitled to. A solicitor can help ensure you receive fair compensation.

Final Thoughts

My cycling accident in Dublin was a challenging experience, but pursuing a personal injury claim helped me recover both physically and financially. While no amount of money can undo the trauma of being knocked down, compensation for medical costs, lost wages, and pain and suffering provided crucial support during my recovery.

If you've been in a similar situation in Ireland, know that you have rights. The legal system is designed to protect accident victims, and with the right legal representation, you can get the compensation you deserve. Don't hesitate to reach out to a qualified personal injury solicitor – they can guide you through the process and ensure your claim is handled properly.

Whether you're a cyclist, pedestrian, or driver involved in an accident, safety should always come first. But if an accident does happen, remember that you're not alone, and there are professionals ready to help you navigate the claims process.
