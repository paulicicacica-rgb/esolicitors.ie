Michal from Cork: Hit by a Van in a Cycle Lane — How He Got Full Compensation

A cyclist's fight for justice after being knocked down by a driver who "didn't see him"
Cork, Ireland

What Happened

Michal was 34 when his life changed in an instant. On a Tuesday morning in Cork city, he was cycling to work on a route he'd taken a hundred times before. He was in a designated cycle lane on the main road—he was doing everything right. The weather was fine, the sun was up, and visibility was clear. He had lights on his bike and wore high-visibility clothing. He felt safe.

Then a white delivery van came around a corner without slowing down. The driver never saw him. The van hit Michal's rear wheel, sending him flying over the handlebars. He landed hard on his left shoulder and hip, skidding across the tarmac. When he came to his senses, people were standing over him. His bike was twisted beyond repair. His shoulder was screaming. He couldn't move his left arm.

The driver got out. He was shaken, apologetic even. "I just didn't see you," he kept saying. "You came from nowhere." But Michal had been right there, in the cycle lane where he was supposed to be. An ambulance came. Hospital tests showed a fractured collarbone, severe soft tissue damage to his shoulder, and cuts and bruising across his side. He was off work for three months. The pain lingered for much longer.

Michal's GP referred him to a solicitor who specialised in personal injury. He felt uncomfortable making a claim—he didn't want to be "that person" who sues. But his solicitor explained that this wasn't about punishment. It was about making sure the person responsible covered the real costs of what they'd done. Lost wages. Hospital bills. The physiotherapy he'd need for months. The fact that he still got nerve pain on damp days, nearly a year later.

The solicitor gathered evidence: the police report, witness statements from people who saw the accident, photographs of the scene, and medical records. They sent a letter of claim to the driver's insurance company. At first, the insurer tried to argue that Michal was partially at fault, that he should have seen the van coming. But the evidence was clear. The driver had failed in his basic duty of care. Within six months, a settlement was agreed: full compensation for all of Michal's losses. He didn't have to go to court.

What the Law Says

Under Irish law, every motorist has a clear legal duty of care to other road users—including cyclists and pedestrians. This is set out in the Road Traffic Act. When a driver hits someone, they must have either acted carelessly or broken the law. In Michal's case, the driver's failure to see him in a clearly marked cycle lane, in good weather and lighting, was negligence.

Specifically, the law says that a motorist must drive at a safe speed for the conditions, keep a proper lookout, and avoid hitting other road users. A driver cannot simply say "I didn't see them" and escape responsibility. That's exactly what they should be doing—seeing what's there. If they fail to do so, they are legally liable for any injury or damage they cause.

In some cases, the local authority that maintains the road or cycle lane can also be liable if poor maintenance, missing signage, or dangerous conditions contributed to the accident. In Michal's case, the cycle lane was well-maintained and clearly marked, so the council had no liability. But in other accidents, they might.

What Michal Was Entitled To

Irish law allows an injured person to claim compensation for several things. First, there's pain and suffering—money that recognises the physical pain, emotional distress, and impact on quality of life. For a serious injury like Michal's fractured collarbone and soft tissue damage, this can range from €10,000 to €30,000 or more, depending on how severe it was and how long it took to recover.

Second, there's loss of earnings. Michal was off work for three months. His solicitor calculated his lost wages and claimed the full amount from the insurer. He also claimed for the cost of private physiotherapy sessions that his GP recommended but that the health service couldn't provide quickly enough.

Third, there are medical and care costs—hospital treatment, ambulance fees, GP visits, prescriptions, and ongoing therapy. His solicitor even claimed for the cost of a replacement bike and for taxi fares during the weeks when he couldn't drive or use public transport due to his injury.

Finally, Michal claimed for future loss. His physiotherapist said he might need ongoing treatment for the next year. Some people experience long-term nerve pain after this type of injury. His solicitor made sure the settlement covered likely future costs, not just what had already happened.

In total, Michal received a settlement of €47,500. This covered all his medical costs, lost earnings, physiotherapy, pain and suffering, and a sum for the lingering pain he still experiences. It wasn't compensation for being hit—it was compensation for the very real losses he had suffered.

⚠️ Important: Time Limits Apply

You have just two years from the date of the accident to make a personal injury claim in Ireland. After that, you lose your right to claim, no matter how strong your case is. If you've been in a cycling or pedestrian accident, speak to a solicitor immediately. Don't wait.
