Cyclist Injured by Pothole: Ibrahim's Compensation Claim from Limerick Council

From Limerick — a claim that proved negligence can lead to fair compensation

What Happened to Ibrahim

Ibrahim had cycled the same route through his Limerick neighbourhood for years without incident. It was a quiet residential street he used almost daily to get to work. He knew every bump and corner. But one morning in March, everything changed.

As he cycled along, his front wheel hit a deep pothole that had formed in the road surface. The pothole was large — big enough that it had caught his wheel and sent him flying over his handlebars. He crashed hard onto the tarmac, landing on his left shoulder and arm. The pain was immediate and severe. A neighbour who witnessed the fall called an ambulance, and Ibrahim was taken to the hospital with a fractured collarbone, severe bruising, and soft tissue damage to his shoulder.

What made this even more frustrating was that Ibrahim discovered the council had been notified about that exact pothole six months earlier. A local resident had reported it as a safety hazard. Six months. Nothing had been done. The pothole had only grown worse with winter weather, and no one had filled it or even cordoned it off.

Ibrahim spent three months in physiotherapy. His arm and shoulder took time to heal. He couldn't cycle for months, and he couldn't do his normal work for weeks. The physical pain was bad enough — but the anger at the negligence was worse. How could a local authority ignore a reported hazard for so long? How was this acceptable?

That's when Ibrahim decided to look into whether he had a legal claim. He contacted eSolicitors.ie and was matched with a personal injury solicitor who specialised in exactly this type of case.

What the Law Says

In Ireland, both motorists and local authorities have legal duties when it comes to the safety of cyclists and pedestrians. Under the Road Traffic Act, everyone who uses the roads — whether in a car, on a bike, or on foot — has the right to a reasonably safe environment.

Local authorities, like Limerick City and County Council, have a specific legal duty to maintain public roads in a safe condition. They have what's called a "duty of care." This means they must inspect roads regularly, identify hazards, and repair them in a reasonable timeframe. They also have a responsibility to respond to reports of problems and to investigate them properly.

When a local authority knows — or should know — about a pothole or other road defect, and fails to repair it within a reasonable time, they can be held liable for injuries that result. The key is that they had actual or constructive notice of the problem. Ibrahim's case was strong because the council had been formally notified six months earlier. They couldn't claim they didn't know.

Ibrahim's solicitor gathered evidence: the original report to the council, photographic evidence of the pothole, witness statements, and his medical records. The council's own records showed they had received the report. The case was straightforward — a known hazard that hadn't been addressed, and a direct injury as a result.

What Ibrahim Was Entitled To

Compensation in cycling accident cases where a local authority is liable typically breaks down into two main areas: damages for pain and suffering, and damages for financial losses (called "special damages").

For pain and suffering, the court considers how serious the injury was, how long recovery took, and what ongoing effects remain. Ibrahim's fractured collarbone, three months of physiotherapy, and the impact on his work and daily life put him in a moderate injury bracket. Compensation for this type of injury in Ireland typically ranges from around €15,000 to €50,000, depending on the specifics.

For financial losses, Ibrahim was entitled to claim for:

• Lost wages during his recovery period
• Cost of physiotherapy and medical treatment
• Any ongoing medical care needed
• Damaged clothing and his bicycle
• Travel costs for medical appointments

In more serious cases — where there's permanent disability, ongoing pain, or major impact on someone's ability to work — compensation can be significantly higher, ranging from €50,000 to €120,000 or more.

Ibrahim's case was settled out of court. The council's insurers accepted liability once they saw the evidence. The settlement included compensation for his pain and suffering, his lost income, all his medical and physiotherapy costs, and the cost of replacing his bike. The total was fair, and it reflected the genuine impact the accident had on his life.

⏰ Important: Time Limits Apply
In Ireland, you have 2 years from the date of the accident to bring a personal injury claim. This is called the "limitation period." After 2 years, you lose the right to claim, no matter how strong your case. If you've been in a cycling or pedestrian accident, don't wait. Contact a solicitor as soon as you can — even if you're still recovering.
