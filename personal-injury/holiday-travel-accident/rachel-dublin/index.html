Rachel's Hotel Pool Slip: How She Won Compensation for Her Ruined Holiday

Dublin, Ireland — A fractured wrist, a wet floor with no warning, and a tour operator held accountable

What Happened

Rachel had been looking forward to her week in the Algarve for months. She'd booked a package holiday with a major Irish tour operator, and everything was arranged — the flights, the hotel, the transfers. She wasn't expecting her holiday to end up in a hospital on day three.

The hotel had a beautiful pool area, and on the afternoon of her third day, Rachel decided to take a dip. As she walked from the sunbeds towards the pool, still wet from the early morning rain that had fallen, her foot caught the slick ceramic tiles beside the deep end. There was no warning sign. No hazard tape. No staff member directing people away from the wet area. She fell hard, her wrist bearing the full impact as she tried to break the fall. The sharp pain told her immediately that something was seriously wrong.

The hotel staff called an ambulance, and Rachel was taken to a local private hospital for X-rays. The diagnosis was a fractured wrist — not a simple crack, but a fracture that required a plaster cast and follow-up physiotherapy. Her holiday, which should have been seven days of relaxation, was over. She spent the next two days in her room, in pain, waiting for a flight home. The remainder of her annual leave was spent recovering, unable to work, unable to drive, unable to do simple things like wash her hair or get dressed without help.

Rachel was understandably frustrated. She'd paid good money for this holiday. The hotel had been negligent — any reasonable establishment would have either dried the floor or put up a warning sign. And her tour operator, the company that had sold her this package, was ultimately responsible for making sure the services they included were safe. She decided to find out if she had a case.

What the Law Says

In Ireland, if you're injured during a package holiday, the responsibility doesn't just sit with the hotel or the local venue. Under the Package Travel Regulations 2019, your tour operator — the company that sold you the package — is legally responsible for the proper performance of all the services that make up your holiday.

This is important. It means Rachel didn't have to chase down a foreign hotel or deal with Spanish courts. Her Irish tour operator was liable because they had a duty of care to ensure that the services they included in their package — including the accommodation — met a reasonable standard of safety.

The regulations also say that the organiser (the tour operator) is liable for damage to persons arising from failure to perform, or improper performance of, the services that form part of the package contract. A wet pool area with no warning sign is a clear failure to maintain a safe environment — it's improper performance of the accommodation service.

What's more, many Irish courts will allow you to bring a claim in Ireland, even though the accident happened abroad. You don't always have to travel to pursue justice.

What She Was Entitled To

When you're injured as a result of someone else's negligence, compensation typically covers several things:

  • Medical expenses: Hospital costs, X-rays, plaster casts, and any treatment you received abroad. Rachel's immediate medical care was covered.
  • Ongoing treatment: Physiotherapy, follow-up appointments, and any further care needed to help you recover. A broken wrist often requires weeks of physio.
  • Lost earnings or lost annual leave: If you can't work because of the injury, you can claim for lost income. Rachel lost the remainder of her holiday and couldn't work immediately after returning home.
  • Pain and suffering: This is the compensation for the injury itself — the pain, the inconvenience, the impact on your daily life. A fractured wrist is a significant injury that affects everything you do.
  • Ruined holiday: You can also claim for the loss of the holiday itself. You paid for a relaxing break and received a hospital visit instead.

In Rachel's case, the tour operator settled her claim for €28,500. This covered all her medical expenses, the physiotherapy she needed over the following three months, the days of work she missed, and compensation for the pain, the discomfort, and the completely ruined holiday.

The amount you can claim varies depending on the severity of your injury, how long recovery takes, your age, and how much of an impact it has on your life. For a fractured wrist similar to Rachel's, settlements typically range from €15,000 to €45,000, though more severe fractures or those with lasting complications can be higher.

⏰ Time Limit Warning

You have only 2 years from the date of your accident to start a personal injury claim. This is called the statute of limitations. In Rachel's case, she had 2 years from the day she slipped by the pool to begin legal proceedings. Some holidays or accidents may have different time limits depending on where they took place — for example, if you're injured in another EU country, sometimes local time limits apply. Don't wait. If you've been injured on holiday, contact a solicitor now to make sure you don't miss your deadline.
