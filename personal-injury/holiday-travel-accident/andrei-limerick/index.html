Scooter Accident on Holiday: How Andrei Won Compensation for Faulty Brakes

Limerick man injured when rental company failed to warn him or check the scooter he hired in Spain

What Happened

Andrei from Limerick was on a week's holiday in the Balearics in August 2022. Like many tourists, he decided to rent a scooter for the day to explore the island. It seemed like a straightforward transaction — he walked into the rental shop, chose a scooter, handed over his passport and credit card, and rode off. No one mentioned checking the brakes. No one told him there was a known fault. He had no idea what he was about to ride into.

Within twenty minutes, while heading down a steep coastal road, Andrei felt the scooter's brakes start to fail. At first it was subtle — a slight sponginess in the lever. Then it got worse. The brakes barely responded. He managed to steer toward the roadside and dumped the scooter, but not before sliding across gravel and hitting a concrete barrier. His left shoulder, arm and ribs took the impact.

He spent two nights in a Spanish hospital with a fractured shoulder, broken ribs and severe road rash across his left side. The hospital bill came to €3,200. When he got home to Ireland, he still needed months of physiotherapy. He couldn't work properly for six weeks. The pain kept him awake at night for months after.

What made it worse was what he found out later. The rental company's own maintenance log — which his solicitor obtained during the claim — showed that the scooter's brakes had been flagged as faulty three weeks before he rented it. They were never fixed. The company had no system for warning customers about problems. It never even occurred to them. To the rental company, a faulty vehicle was just part of the job.

Andrei wasn't just injured. He was angry. And he wanted to know if he had any comeback against the people who put him on a defective scooter without a word of warning.

What the Law Says

Here's the thing about travel accidents: if you book your holiday through a tour operator or travel agent, Irish law gives you strong protection even if the accident happens abroad.

The Package Travel Regulations 2019 say that a tour operator is legally responsible for the proper performance of all services included in your package holiday — including vehicle rentals. This means the tour operator can't just wash their hands of what happens. If you're injured because of a faulty service, the operator is liable.

In Andrei's case, the tour operator had arranged the scooter rental as part of his package. That meant the operator was legally on the hook. The rental company was also liable for negligence — they failed to maintain their vehicle and failed to warn a customer about a known defect.

Good news: you can usually bring a claim in Ireland even though the accident happened overseas. You don't have to hire a Spanish lawyer or travel back to Spain to fight through the Spanish court system. Irish courts will often hear these cases, and Irish law can apply — especially if you booked through an Irish tour operator or travel agent.

What Andrei Was Entitled To

Compensation for a holiday injury has two main parts:

General damages (pain, suffering, lost quality of life)

This is for the physical injury itself — the pain, the scarring, the months of restricted movement. For Andrei's fractured shoulder and broken ribs, with ongoing pain and physiotherapy, this came to €18,500. The injury was serious but wasn't permanently disabling.

Special damages (out-of-pocket costs)

This covers the money Andrei actually spent:

  • Hospital bill: €3,200
  • Physiotherapy in Ireland: €1,850
  • Lost wages during recovery (6 weeks off work): €3,400
  • Travel and accommodation to Spain for medical follow-ups: €620
  • Prescription painkillers and equipment: €280

Total special damages: €9,350

Final settlement: €28,100

The settlement was paid by the tour operator's insurance and the rental company's insurance working together. Andrei didn't have to go to court; both insurers agreed liability was clear once they saw the maintenance records.

⚠️ Don't Wait — There's a Time Limit

You usually have 2 years from the date of the accident to bring a personal injury claim.

This deadline can vary depending on where the accident happened and which law applies to your case. Some countries have shorter limits. Some have longer ones. But in general, 2 years is the Irish standard — and that clock starts ticking the moment you're injured.

Don't assume you have more time. Contact a solicitor as soon as you can. If you wait until month 23, you might find that your right to claim has expired and you've lost everything. Gathering evidence, obtaining medical records, and building a case takes time.

The longer you wait, the harder it gets. Witnesses disappear. Memories fade. Records get lost. Medical evidence is fresher if gathered soon after the accident.

Similar Cases

Andrei's situation isn't unique. Here are three other cases we've seen:

Sophie from Dublin — Boat Rental Accident (Greek Islands)

Rented a speedboat through a travel company. Engine failed mid-journey, boat drifted into rocks. Sophie suffered cuts, a sprained wrist, and concussion. The rental company had skipped safety checks. The tour operator paid €22,600 in settlement, covering hospital costs, lost wages, and pain and suffering.

Marcus from Cork — Climbing Equipment Failure (French Alps)

Paid for a guided climbing experience through a tour operator. Harness had a faulty buckle that came loose partway up. Marcus fell 8 feet, broke his leg in two places. He needed surgery and months of recovery. Both the guide company and tour operator were liable. Settlement: €35,400.

Elena from Galway — Jet Ski Collision (Portugal)

Jet ski rental provided no safety briefing and no helmet check. Another renter collided with Elena's jet ski. She sustained facial injuries and needed plastic surgery. The rental company and tour operator both settled for €41,200 combined.
