Hospitalised with Food Poisoning at an All-Inclusive Resort — Tadgh's Claim for Compensation

Tadgh, Cork | Holiday Accident | €45,000 Compensation Awarded

What Happened

Tadgh from Cork was looking forward to a week away with his family at an all-inclusive resort in Spain. He'd booked through a major Irish tour operator, paid in full, and had been counting down the days. The resort looked beautiful in the photos online, and the price seemed reasonable for everything included — flights, hotel, meals, the lot. When they arrived, the resort seemed nice enough on first impression.

By the second day of their holiday, Tadgh started to feel unwell. He had severe stomach cramps, nausea, and vomiting. At first, he thought it might just be travel sickness or a reaction to the change in food, but it got worse. That evening, three other guests from the same tour group also became ill with the same symptoms. By the next morning, Tadgh was so poorly that he couldn't leave his room. His family made the difficult decision to take him to the local hospital.

The hospital confirmed that Tadgh had severe bacterial food poisoning. He was admitted and spent three days being treated with IV fluids and antibiotics. The doctors told him that multiple guests at the resort had presented with the same infection, suggesting a serious problem with food hygiene or preparation at the hotel. Tadgh missed the rest of his holiday in a hospital bed, feeling miserable and worried about the costs piling up. His family had to cut their trip short to take him home.

When Tadgh got home to Cork, he took time off work to recover. He had medical bills from his treatment abroad, and he felt exhausted for weeks afterwards. He wasn't just upset about the ruined holiday — he'd paid good money for a break that ended up making him seriously ill. That's when he decided to look into whether he had any legal rights.

Tadgh contacted eSolicitors.ie and spoke to a personal injury solicitor. The solicitor explained that tour operators have a legal duty to make sure that all the services they provide — including the accommodation and food — are safe and properly managed. The fact that multiple guests had got sick from the same cause suggested the hotel had failed in its duty of care. Within months, Tadgh's solicitor had helped him file a claim against the tour operator, and a settlement was agreed.

What the Law Says About Holiday Accidents

Package Travel Regulations 2019

Under Irish law, specifically the Package Travel Regulations 2019, a tour operator is fully responsible for the proper performance of all services that form part of a holiday package — including accommodation, meals, transport, and activities. This means that if you book an all-inclusive holiday through an Irish operator, they are legally liable for what happens to you during that holiday, even if the resort or airline is located abroad.

If a tour operator fails to provide these services to a proper standard — for example, if food is contaminated or hygiene is poor — and you suffer injury or illness as a result, you have the right to claim compensation. The tour operator cannot simply blame the foreign hotel; under Irish law, they are the ones ultimately responsible to you.

This is important because it means you don't have to chase a foreign company through foreign courts. You can bring your claim in Ireland against the Irish tour operator who sold you the package.

What Tadgh Was Entitled To

Compensation Breakdown

  • General Damages (Pain and Suffering): €20,000 — for the pain, discomfort, and distress caused by three days of severe food poisoning and hospitalisation
  • Medical Costs: €8,500 — for the hospital treatment, medications, and medical consultations abroad
  • Lost Holiday: €10,000 — compensation for the cost of the holiday days he couldn't enjoy due to being ill
  • Loss of Earnings: €4,200 — for the time he had to take off work to recover
  • Travel Expenses: €2,300 — for the cost of getting home early and additional medical travel

Total Compensation Awarded: €45,000

The compensation reflected not just the financial costs Tadgh had to pay out, but also the very real suffering he experienced. Food poisoning severe enough to require hospitalisation is serious, and the court recognised that Tadgh had lost his holiday, missed work, and endured weeks of recovery. The fact that the tour operator had a duty of care and failed to ensure the resort met basic food safety standards meant they were liable for the full range of damages.

⚠️ Important: Time Limits Apply

If you've suffered an injury or illness during a holiday, you generally have two years from the date of the accident to bring a legal claim in Ireland. However, this time limit can vary depending on where the accident occurred and the specific circumstances. Some claims must be started within one year, while others may have longer periods — but two years is the most common deadline.

Do not wait. Once the time limit has passed, you lose your legal right to compensation, even if you have a strong case. If you've had an accident on holiday, contact a solicitor as soon as you can to protect your rights.
