Miriam's Holiday Injury in Galway — How She Won Her Claim Against the Tour Operator

An excursion with no safety checks. Injuries that changed her summer. A settlement that made things right.

What Happened

Miriam had been looking forward to her summer holiday for months. She'd booked a package deal through a well-known Irish tour operator — a two-week trip that included accommodation, transfers, and several excursions. On the fifth day, she joined a guided activity advertised as a "scenic coastal adventure" run by a local provider at her destination.

When she arrived at the meeting point, there was no safety briefing. No one checked her fitness level. No one explained the risks or showed her how to use any of the equipment properly. The guide simply gathered everyone together and started the activity within minutes. Miriam noticed that some of the gear looked worn and poorly maintained, but she assumed the professionals knew what they were doing.

Halfway through the excursion, Miriam slipped on an uneven section. She fell hard onto her left arm and shoulder, fracturing her wrist and dislocating her shoulder. The guide radioed for help, but getting proper medical care was complicated and expensive. Even after returning to Ireland, she needed months of physiotherapy and was unable to work for nearly four months.

Miriam felt angry and let down. She hadn't just paid for a holiday — she'd paid for what she believed was a safe, properly run activity. Instead, she was left with pain, bills, and lost income. That's when she decided to find a solicitor who could help her understand her rights.

With legal support, Miriam pursued a claim against the tour operator under Irish law. Her solicitor gathered evidence showing that basic safety procedures hadn't been followed and that the equipment hadn't been properly maintained. The tour operator's own liability insurance eventually settled the claim before it went to court, recognising their failure to provide proper safeguards.

What the Law Says

In Ireland, tour operators are bound by the Package Travel Regulations 2019. These rules are strict and clear: if you book a package holiday (accommodation plus activities, or transfers plus activities), the tour operator is legally responsible for making sure every part of that package is performed properly and safely.

This means that even if the actual activity is run by a local provider abroad, your tour operator in Ireland can't just wash their hands of it. They have a duty to:

  • Choose activity providers carefully and check their safety standards
  • Make sure proper safety briefings and equipment checks happen
  • Ensure all activities comply with local and international safety rules
  • Have proper insurance in place

If they fail in any of these duties and you're injured as a result, they are liable. You can bring a claim in Ireland even if the injury happened abroad. The courts recognise that Irish consumers booking through Irish companies should have Irish legal protection.

What Miriam Was Entitled To

When a personal injury claim like Miriam's is settled or won in court, compensation covers several things:

  • General Damages: For pain, suffering, and loss of enjoyment of life
  • Special Damages: For financial losses like medical bills, travel costs, and lost wages
  • Future Care Costs: If the injury requires ongoing treatment or support
  • Loss of Earnings: Both past loss and future earning capacity if the injury affects work

Miriam's claim included all of these elements. The holiday was meant to be a restorative break, and the accident not only caused physical injury but also prevented her from enjoying the time she'd paid for and taken off work.
