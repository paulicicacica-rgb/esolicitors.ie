How Siobhan Won Her Case After Slipping in a Dublin Lidl

From denial to settlement: a fractured wrist, lost wages, and €28,000 in compensation

Siobhan's Story

It was a Tuesday afternoon in March when everything changed. What should have been a quick shop turned into a medical emergency and a fight for justice.

Siobhan had popped into her local Lidl on the northside of Dublin to grab a few things for dinner. She'd been in that shop dozens of times before, and she didn't think twice as she walked down the aisle towards the refrigerated section. But as her foot hit the wet floor near the dairy display, her leg just went from under her. She landed hard on her wrist, and the pain was immediate and searing.

Other shoppers and staff rushed over. A manager appeared within minutes, apologising profusely. He admitted that a refrigerator door had been leaking water onto the floor. There was no wet floor sign anywhere nearby, no cone, nothing to warn customers. The staff were genuinely sorry, and Siobhan remembers thinking that would be the end of it. They'd clearly messed up, she'd been hurt, and they'd help her sort it out.

But it wasn't that simple. An ambulance took her to hospital, where X-rays revealed a fractured wrist. She needed a cast for eight weeks, and then months of physiotherapy. She couldn't work—Siobhan is a graphic designer, and she needed both hands to do her job. Weeks turned into months, and the medical bills kept coming. Physio sessions, GP appointments, the time off work without pay. The stress of it all was overwhelming.

When she finally contacted the shop's insurance company, they flat-out denied liability. They claimed she might have been walking too quickly, or that there was adequate signage, or any number of other things. Despite the manager's apology on the day, Lidl's legal team wasn't playing ball. That's when Siobhan knew she needed proper legal advice. She couldn't fight this alone.

What the Law Says

The Occupiers Liability Act 1995

In Ireland, shop owners and property managers have a legal duty of care to anyone who visits their premises. This is set out in the Occupiers Liability Act 1995.

That duty means they must take reasonable steps to keep their premises safe. If there's a hazard—like a wet floor—they must either fix it, clean it up, or warn people about it clearly. A wet floor sign, a cone, staff watching the area: these are the kinds of basic precautions that would satisfy that duty.

When a business fails in that duty, and someone is injured as a result, they can be held liable for compensation. It doesn't matter whether the manager was nice about it or apologised on the day. The law is clear: if you create a dangerous condition and don't warn people about it, you're responsible for the harm that follows.

In Siobhan's case, the evidence was strong. A leaking fridge, a wet floor, no warning—that's a straightforward breach of duty. The shop should have spotted that leak, mopped it up, and put a sign there. They didn't, and Siobhan paid the price.

What Siobhan Was Entitled To

Once Siobhan had proper legal representation, the picture became much clearer. The shop's insurance company eventually realised they were going to lose if this went to court, so they agreed to settle.

Siobhan's Settlement Breakdown

  • Pain and suffering (general damages): €12,000 for the fractured wrist, eight weeks in a cast, months of pain and physiotherapy.
  • Lost wages (special damages): €10,500 for the four and a half months she couldn't work as a designer.
  • Medical expenses: €3,200 for hospital, GP visits, physio sessions, and other related costs.
  • Travel costs: €1,300 for taxis to and from appointments when she couldn't drive.
  • Additional recovery costs: €1,000 for equipment and support to help her get back to work.

Total settlement: €28,000

This covered not just the immediate injury, but the real-world impact: lost income, ongoing medical care, the practical difficulties of recovering from a serious wrist injury. Siobhan's life had been disrupted for months, and this settlement reflected that.
