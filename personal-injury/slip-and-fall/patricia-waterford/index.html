Patricia's Slip and Fall at a Waterford Restaurant

How an unmarked step and poor lighting led to a successful claim

What Happened

Patricia had been looking forward to a nice evening out. She'd booked a table at a well-known restaurant in Waterford city centre with friends. It was a Friday night, and the place was busy and warm. When she arrived, she pushed through the entrance doors, but what should have been a straightforward walk to the restaurant floor turned into a nightmare.

There was an unmarked step down at the entrance — one of those internal steps that separates the porch area from the main floor. In the dim lighting of the entrance hall, Patricia simply didn't see it coming. Her foot caught, and she fell hard onto her left knee and hand. Another customer helped her up, and although she was shaken and hurt, she managed to get through the evening, not wanting to make a fuss. It was only when she got home that she realized how serious the injury was.

Over the next few days, the pain got worse. A visit to her GP led to a referral to hospital, where scans revealed a cracked patella — a fracture in her kneecap. What had felt like a stumble in a restaurant was actually a significant injury that would need months of physiotherapy and would keep her off work for over three months. Patricia worked as a hairdresser, and the physical demands of standing all day made her recovery even more complicated.

Six weeks into her recovery, Patricia contacted eSolicitors.ie. She wasn't sure if she had a case, but something about the whole thing felt wrong to her. Why wasn't that step marked? Why was the lighting so poor? Her solicitor advised her that she very likely did have a strong claim, and explained why.

What the Law Says

The Occupiers Liability Act 1995

In Ireland, businesses and property owners have a legal duty of care to anyone who visits their premises. This duty is set out in the Occupiers Liability Act 1995.

Under this law, an occupier (like a restaurant owner) must keep their premises safe. They must:

  • Fix or clearly warn about hazards like steps, wet floors, or obstacles
  • Provide adequate lighting in areas where people walk
  • Keep common areas clean and free from dangers
  • Take reasonable steps to prevent injuries

If an occupier fails in this duty and someone is injured as a result, they can be held liable for damages.

In Patricia's case, the restaurant had failed on multiple counts. The step at the entrance was unmarked — there was no high-visibility tape, no painted edge, nothing to warn visitors that the level was changing. The lighting was poor, making it even harder to spot the hazard. A reasonable restaurant owner should have either marked that step clearly or ensured the lighting was bright enough for customers to see it.

When Patricia's solicitor sent a letter before action to the restaurant, setting out the facts and the law, the restaurant's insurance company didn't contest the claim. They knew they were at fault. Within months, Patricia had her settlement.

What Patricia Received

The Settlement

Total compensation: €28,500

This included:

  • €15,000 for pain and suffering from the fracture and recovery period
  • €8,200 for loss of earnings during her three months off work
  • €3,800 for physiotherapy and medical costs
  • €1,500 for travel to hospital appointments and loss of other expenses

Patricia's compensation reflected the real impact the accident had on her life — not just the immediate medical costs, but the months she couldn't work, the pain she went through, and the money she spent on her own recovery. Every aspect of her loss was considered.

Typical Compensation for Slip and Fall Injuries

Compensation in slip and fall cases depends on several factors:

  • The severity of injury: A minor sprain might be worth €5,000–€15,000, while a fracture or long-term injury could be worth €20,000–€60,000 or more
  • Time off work: If you had to stop earning money while you recovered, that loss is compensated in full
  • Medical and therapy costs: All reasonable expenses are covered
  • Ongoing effects: If the injury causes lasting pain or disability, this is reflected in a higher award
  • How clear the occupier's fault was: If liability is obvious (as it was in Patricia's case), the claim moves faster and settlement is more likely

Important: Two-Year Time Limit

You have two years from the date of your accident to issue legal proceedings. This is a strict deadline under Irish law. If you don't start your claim within two years, you may lose the right to compensation entirely, even if you have a strong case.

This doesn't mean you have to go to court — most claims settle before that. But you do need to contact a solicitor and get the claim process started within the two-year window.
