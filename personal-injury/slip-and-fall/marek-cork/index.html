Marek Wins Compensation After Tripping on Broken Floor Tile in Cork Shopping Centre

How one man's fall led to a successful injury claim
Cork

What Happened to Marek

Marek was doing his usual Saturday shopping at a busy shopping centre in Cork when his foot caught on something underfoot. He looked down and saw a broken floor tile, cracked right through the middle with a sharp edge sticking up. He'd tripped hard and fallen forward, catching himself on his hands to break the fall. His wrist took the impact, and he felt immediate pain.

Shaken and hurting, Marek reported the hazard to centre security straight away. He wanted them to know what had caused his fall and to make sure no one else had the same accident. The security officer told him they'd only noticed the broken tile that morning — but hadn't yet cordoned it off or put up warning signs. Despite knowing about the damage, they'd left it exposed for shoppers to encounter.

Over the following weeks, Marek's wrist didn't improve as expected. He needed physiotherapy and had to take time off work, which meant losing income on top of his medical bills. The shopping centre wasn't responding to his attempts to discuss what had happened, and he knew he needed proper legal advice.

When Marek came to his solicitor, they explained that the shopping centre had a legal duty to keep their premises safe. They'd failed that duty by leaving a hazard unprotected, especially after becoming aware of it. The broken tile wasn't an accident — it was negligence. Marek deserved compensation not just for his injuries, but for the wages he'd lost and the costs of treatment.

With legal backing, the shopping centre's insurers eventually settled. Marek received compensation that covered his medical expenses, his lost earnings, and pain and suffering. The settlement recognized that the shopping centre had failed in their basic responsibility to keep their customers safe.

What the Law Says

The Occupiers Liability Act 1995

In Ireland, the Occupiers Liability Act 1995 is clear: anyone who owns or controls a building — a shop, office, restaurant, or public space — has a legal duty to make sure it's safe for visitors.

This means the property owner must:

  • Keep the premises in a reasonably safe condition
  • Warn visitors about any hazards they know about
  • Carry out regular checks for damage and wear
  • Fix problems quickly, or fence them off until they're fixed

When a property owner breaches this duty — by ignoring a hazard, not fixing a known problem, or failing to warn people — they're legally liable for injuries that result. In Marek's case, the shopping centre knew about the broken tile but did nothing to protect shoppers. That's a clear breach of their duty of care.

What Marek Was Entitled To

Marek's compensation award covered three main areas:

Medical Costs and Treatment

All physiotherapy sessions, GP visits, and any specialist consultations needed to treat his wrist injury.

Lost Earnings

The weeks Marek couldn't work while recovering were covered — his employer wouldn't pay him, so the compensation made up that lost income.

Pain and Suffering

Compensation for the physical pain, discomfort, and disruption to his life while he healed. This varied based on how severe the injury was and how long recovery took.

In cases like Marek's — a fall causing a wrist injury with some lost work time — compensation typically ranges from €10,000 to €60,000, depending on how serious the injury is, how much income was lost, and any lasting effects. Marek's settlement fell within this range, reflecting the impact on his work and recovery time.

⏰ Important: Time Limit for Your Claim

You have 2 years from the date of your accident to start a personal injury claim.

This deadline is not flexible — once 2 years have passed, you lose the right to claim, even if you have a strong case. If you've had a slip, trip, or fall in a shop, shopping centre, or public place, don't delay. Contact a solicitor as soon as possible to protect your rights.
