Back Injury at Work in Cork: How Olga Got Compensation Without Proper Training

A warehouse worker injured lifting heavy boxes with no manual handling training. Her employer said it was "just part of the job." Here's how she settled her case.

Olga's Story

Olga had worked as a warehouse assistant in Cork for three years. She was good at her job, quick, and reliable. But in November 2022, everything changed in just a few seconds.

That morning, she was told to move a stack of heavy boxes from one section of the warehouse to another. There was no forklift available, and nobody suggested using one. Nobody had ever shown her the right way to lift heavy items—no training, no guidance, nothing. She'd just watched other workers do it and copied them. When she bent down to pick up a particularly heavy box, she felt a sharp pain shoot through her lower back. She managed to finish her shift, but by evening she could barely move.

Over the next few weeks, Olga struggled. She went to her GP, who referred her to a physiotherapist. The pain didn't go away. She had to take time off work, and when she did come back, she couldn't do her normal duties. She was moved to lighter tasks, but at reduced pay. Her line manager told her that back injuries were common in warehouse work and that "it's just part of the job." When she asked HR about reporting the injury formally or getting support, they brushed her off. Nobody mentioned her rights or what she might be entitled to.

By early 2023, Olga realised this wasn't going to get better on its own. She was still in pain, she'd lost income, and she felt let down by her employer. A friend suggested she speak to a solicitor about a personal injury claim. That's when things finally started to move forward.

After gathering evidence—her medical records, photos of the workspace, and witness statements from colleagues—her solicitor sent a letter of claim to her employer's insurance company. The evidence was clear: the company had failed in its legal duty to provide safe working conditions and proper training. Within six months, the case was settled. Olga received €47,500, which covered her ongoing treatment, loss of earnings during recovery, and compensation for her pain and suffering.

What the Law Says About Your Safety at Work

In Ireland, your employer has a legal duty to protect your health and safety. This isn't optional—it's the law.

The Safety, Health and Welfare at Work Act 2005

This is the main law that protects workers in Ireland. It says that employers must:

  • Provide a safe workplace, equipment, and systems of work
  • Give you information, instruction, and training about safety
  • Protect your health and safety, not just follow rules on paper
  • Consult with workers about safety matters
  • Make sure the workplace is properly maintained and safe

When an employer doesn't do these things, and you get injured as a result, they are negligent. That negligence can form the basis of a personal injury claim.

Manual Handling Training is Mandatory

If your job involves lifting, carrying, or moving anything—boxes, equipment, stock, or anything else—your employer must provide you with proper manual handling training. They cannot assume you'll just pick it up by watching others. They cannot tell you it's "just part of the job" as an excuse not to train you. And they cannot ignore safety just because a task is routine.

Your Right to Report an Injury

You have the right to report a workplace injury to your employer. They must take it seriously, record it properly, and not dismiss you or punish you for reporting. If they do, that's also illegal.

What Compensation Typically Looks Like

Personal injury compensation for a workplace back injury usually covers three main things:

Medical and Healthcare Costs
This includes physiotherapy, ongoing treatment, GP visits, and any future medical care you'll need. Olga's settlement covered six months of physiotherapy and ongoing monitoring.
Loss of Earnings
If you've had to take time off work or move to a lower-paid role because of your injury, you're entitled to that money back. This includes wages you lost while off sick and any reduction in pay during recovery.
Pain and Suffering
This covers the pain, distress, and impact on your quality of life. A back injury that causes chronic pain is worth more than a minor strain that heals quickly. This depends on how long your recovery takes and how much the injury affects your daily life.

What's the Range?

For a workplace back injury with ongoing impact and employer negligence, settlements typically range from €20,000 to €150,000 or more. Factors that affect the amount include:

  • How severe the injury is
  • Whether you'll have long-term pain or disability
  • How much time you've lost from work
  • How clear the employer's negligence is
  • Your age and ability to return to work
  • The strength of your medical evidence

Olga's settlement of €47,500 reflected a moderate back injury with several months of recovery, significant loss of earnings, and clear negligence by her employer (no training provided, no safe systems in place).

⚠️ Time Limit: Don't Wait

You have 2 years from the date of your injury to start a personal injury claim.

After 2 years, your right to claim expires. If you were injured at work, don't assume your employer will sort it. Don't wait to see if your injury gets better on its own. Contact a solicitor as soon as you can. Most offer free initial consultations, so there's no cost to getting advice.
