When a Warehouse Rack Collapsed: Blessing's Story

Dublin | Workplace Injury Claim | Successful Outcome

Blessing worked as a warehouse operator in Dublin for five years. The job was straightforward—moving stock, organising shelves, keeping the place running smoothly. One afternoon, while he was reaching for boxes on a tall metal storage rack, the whole structure gave way without warning. Boxes tumbled down, and Blessing was trapped underneath. His colleagues pulled him out quickly, but the damage was done. He had a broken arm, several bruised ribs, and an injury to his shoulder that would take months to heal. He spent three weeks off work and months in physiotherapy.

When Blessing reported the injury to his employer, he expected to move through the standard process. But when he asked about maintenance records for the rack—something that should have been routine—the company couldn't produce them. They claimed the rack had been inspected recently, but there was nothing on file. No dates, no signatures, no evidence that anyone had ever actually checked whether it was safe. For Blessing, this was deeply frustrating. He had followed the rules and reported everything properly, but now he was left with ongoing pain and questions about whether his employer had been negligent.

Blessing decided to pursue a workplace injury claim. The lack of inspection records was significant. Employers have a legal responsibility to maintain their equipment and prove they've done so. When a company can't show that it's looked after its facilities properly, that's a serious problem. His legal team gathered medical evidence of his injuries, documented his lost wages and ongoing treatment costs, and highlighted the employer's failure to maintain basic safety records. The case was strong, and the employer eventually settled. Blessing received compensation that covered his medical expenses, lost income, and the ongoing costs of his injury.

What the Law Says

In Ireland, employers must provide a safe workplace and maintain all equipment properly. This is outlined in the Safety, Health and Welfare at Work Act 2005. If someone is injured because equipment wasn't maintained or was unsafe, and the employer cannot prove they looked after it, the injured worker has grounds for a claim. The employer's failure to keep records of inspections or maintenance strengthens the worker's position, as it shows they didn't take their legal obligations seriously.

Important Time Limits

⚠️ Act Now: Time Limits Apply

You have three years from the date of your injury to make a claim for a workplace accident in Ireland. This deadline is strict. If you miss it, you lose your right to claim, no matter how strong your case is. If you were injured at work, do not wait. Contact a solicitor as soon as possible to protect your legal rights and gather evidence while everything is fresh.
