Brendan's Fall from Scaffolding: A Dublin Construction Site Negligence Case

How one worker held his employer accountable after a preventable accident

What Happened

Brendan had been working in construction for fifteen years. On a Tuesday morning in March, he was assigned to the sixth-floor scaffolding on a new apartment block in Dublin's Northside. It was routine work — the kind he'd done hundreds of times before. But that day, something went wrong.

As he reached for a piece of formwork, his foot caught on a scaffold board that had come loose. He lost his balance and fell. No harness. No netting below. Just a long drop and then the sound of his body hitting the concrete floor below.

Brendan spent three weeks in hospital. Two broken ribs, a fractured collarbone, a punctured lung, and injuries to his left leg. The physical pain was real. So was the mental toll. When he was finally discharged, he couldn't sleep without nightmares. The thought of climbing back onto any scaffold made him feel sick.

His employer's response was cold. They said it was Brendan's responsibility to ask for a safety harness if he wanted one. It was there on site, they claimed — he just hadn't requested it. The implication was clear: this was his fault. But Brendan knew that wasn't right. He'd never been shown where the harnesses were kept. He'd never been given a toolbox talk about fall protection. And most importantly, he'd never been told that he could request one.

That's when Brendan contacted a solicitor. Within weeks, it became clear that his employer had broken the law. Not just in spirit, but in letter. A safety audit of the site revealed no formal system for fall protection at all. The harnesses existed, yes, but there was no training, no supervision, and no way for workers to know they should use them. When Brendan's solicitor dug deeper, they found that the site's own safety policy had been signed but never distributed. His employer had cut corners on safety because it was cheaper.

What the Law Says

The Safety, Health and Welfare at Work Act 2005

Irish law is very clear: employers have a statutory duty to provide a safe workplace. This isn't optional. It isn't something workers need to ask for. It's a legal requirement that sits with the employer.

Under the Act, employers must:

  • Provide safe equipment — and ensure it's fit for purpose
  • Give proper training — so workers know how to use safety equipment safely
  • Supervise — to make sure safety rules are actually being followed
  • Assess risks — and document what they've found
  • Inform workers — clearly and openly about hazards and how to protect themselves

Brendan's employer failed on all five counts. They had harnesses on site but didn't train workers on them. They didn't tell workers those harnesses existed. They didn't supervise their use. And they blamed the worker when something went wrong.

The law recognises that workers can't be expected to protect themselves from hazards they don't know exist. That's the employer's job. The employer is the one with the knowledge, the resources, and the responsibility.

What Brendan Was Entitled To

Because Brendan's injuries were significant and his employer's negligence was clear, he received full compensation. Here's what that looked like:

  • General damages for pain and suffering: €35,000 — this covers the physical pain, the hospital time, the ongoing discomfort, and the emotional trauma of the accident itself
  • Special damages for lost earnings: €24,000 — Brendan was off work for four months, and this compensates for the money he lost
  • Future loss of earnings: €18,000 — Brendan now tires more easily and can't work full-time anymore; this covers the gap between what he used to earn and what he can earn now
  • Cost of medical care and treatment: €7,500 — physiotherapy, GP visits, medications, and ongoing monitoring
  • Aggravated damages: €15,000 — because the employer's response was dismissive and added to Brendan's distress

Total award: €99,500

It's important to understand that this compensation wasn't punishment — it was restoration. It put Brendan back into the financial position he would have been in if the accident hadn't happened. It paid for his medical care. It replaced the income he lost. And it acknowledged the pain and suffering he endured.

The range for workplace injury claims in Ireland typically runs from €20,000 to €150,000 or more, depending on how severe the injury is, how long recovery takes, and whether the injury affects your ability to work long-term. Brendan's case fell firmly in the middle-to-upper range because his injuries were serious and long-lasting.

⚠️ Time Limit: Don't Wait

You have two years from the date of your injury to make a personal injury claim in Ireland. After that, your right to claim is gone. There are some exceptions, but they're rare. If you've been injured at work, get legal advice immediately — don't assume you're too injured to deal with it now. A solicitor can help you gather evidence while it's fresh and while witnesses still remember what happened.
