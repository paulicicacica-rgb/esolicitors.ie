Factory Worker's Hand Crushed in Unguarded Machinery — Limerick Workplace Injury

James from Limerick — Permanent hand injury caused by missing safety guard

What Happened to James

James had worked on the factory floor in Limerick for almost eight years. He knew the machines, knew the rhythms of the shifts, and took pride in being reliable and careful. But on an ordinary Tuesday morning in March, everything changed in a moment that lasted less than a second.

The machine James worked with had a safety guard that prevented workers from accidentally putting their hands near the moving parts. It was a simple piece of equipment — a metal cage that had been fitted years ago for exactly this reason. Three weeks before the accident, the guard had been removed for scheduled maintenance. The factory maintenance team was supposed to reinstall it. They didn't. No one seemed to be checking. No one seemed to remember that the work was incomplete.

That morning, James was feeding material into the machine when his attention was drawn away for just a moment by a question from a colleague standing nearby. His hand drifted into the gap where the guard should have been. The machinery caught his right hand and crushed it before he could pull away. The pain was instant and overwhelming. When they rushed him to the hospital, the doctors told him what he'd already feared — the damage was permanent. Two of his fingers would never work properly again. His grip strength was reduced. The fine motor control he'd always had was gone.

James couldn't return to his old job. His employer offered him a desk role at significantly lower pay, but even that proved difficult because the pain and reduced function in his hand made typing and holding tools uncomfortable. He had to take extended time off work. Medical bills mounted. He worried about his future — how would he pay his mortgage? Could he ever work a full-time job again? Would the pain ever fully go away?

That's when James contacted eSolicitors.ie. He was unsure if he had a case, and he certainly didn't know the law. But he knew one thing: the guard had been missing. He knew no one had told him it was off the machine. And he knew his hand would never be the same.

What the Law Says About Workplace Safety

In Ireland, your employer has a legal duty to keep you safe at work. This is set out in the Safety, Health and Welfare at Work Act 2005, which is the main law that protects workers like James.

Under this law, employers must:

  • Provide safe equipment and machinery with proper guards and protective devices
  • Maintain that equipment regularly so it stays safe
  • Tell workers about hazards and how to avoid them
  • Provide training so workers know how to use equipment safely
  • Fix problems as soon as they discover them

What went wrong in James's case: The safety guard was removed and not replaced. For three weeks, the machine was dangerous. James wasn't told the guard was missing. His employer failed to maintain safe equipment and failed to tell him about the hazard. This is a clear breach of their legal duty.

The law also says that if an employer breaks this duty and someone gets hurt as a result, that person can claim compensation. This is called a "negligence claim" — it means the employer was careless and that carelessness caused injury.

What James Was Entitled To

Once James's solicitor gathered the evidence — medical records, witness statements from colleagues, proof that the guard had been removed, records showing it was never reinstalled, and documentation that James hadn't been warned — it became clear that the factory's liability was strong.

In cases like James's, compensation falls into two main categories:

General Damages (for pain, suffering, and loss of quality of life)

This covers the physical pain, the emotional distress, the loss of enjoyment in life, and the permanent reduction in quality of life. For a serious hand injury with permanent loss of function, this typically ranges from €15,000 to €50,000, depending on how much function was lost and how much it affects daily life.

Special Damages (for measurable financial losses)

This covers things you can add up: medical and rehabilitation costs, lost wages (both from time off work and reduced earning capacity in the future), equipment or home modifications needed because of the injury. For James, this included hospital stays, physiotherapy, time off work, and the difference between his old salary and what he could now earn. This typically ranges from €5,000 to €100,000+, depending on how much future earning capacity was lost.

James's settlement: Because of the seriousness of his injury (permanent loss of function in two fingers), the clear negligence of his employer, the ongoing medical needs, and his reduced ability to earn a living going forward, James's case was valued at €87,000. This reflected both his immediate losses and his long-term reduced earning capacity.
