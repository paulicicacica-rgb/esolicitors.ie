How Yvonne Won Compensation After Slipping in a Hotel Kitchen in Galway

She asked for safe footwear repeatedly. When the hotel refused to provide it, she slipped on a wet floor and fractured her ankle. Here's how she claimed back what she was owed.

Yvonne's Story

Yvonne had worked as a commis chef at a busy four-star hotel in Galway for three years. She loved the job — the pace, the teamwork, the satisfaction of cooking good food. But the hotel kitchen was always wet. Water from the dishwashers, steam from the pots, cleaning spillages — it was slippery underfoot almost every shift.

About six months before her accident, Yvonne started asking the head chef for non-slip kitchen clogs. She mentioned it casually at first, then more formally. She filled in a workplace concern form. Nothing happened. Other hotels she'd worked in had proper non-slip footwear as standard. She knew it was a simple safety measure. But the hotel's response was always the same: "We can't afford to provide those. Just be careful."

On a Thursday afternoon in October, Yvonne was carrying a tray of hot plates from the prep station towards the pass. Her regular work shoes — ordinary leather pumps from a high street shop — had no grip on the wet floor. Her foot slipped. She went down hard, her left ankle twisting beneath her as she fell. The pain was immediate and severe. One of her colleagues called an ambulance.

At the hospital, the X-rays showed a complete fracture of her left ankle. She needed surgery and spent three months in a walking boot, then months in physiotherapy. She couldn't work. She couldn't afford her mortgage payments and her car loan without her wages. And even after she healed, she found she couldn't stand for eight-hour shifts without her ankle aching. The job she loved became painful. She ended up taking a lower-paid office role.

That's when Yvonne contacted a solicitor. She'd read about workplace injury claims and realised the hotel had broken the law. They knew about the hazard. They'd had multiple requests for safety equipment. They'd done nothing. With proper legal advice, Yvonne decided to claim.

What the Law Says

In Ireland, the Safety, Health and Welfare at Work Act 2005 is clear: employers have a legal duty to keep their workplace safe. This isn't optional. It's a law.

The employer's duty includes:

• Providing safe equipment and protective gear — including appropriate footwear in hazardous environments

• Maintaining a safe workplace free from foreseeable hazards

• Providing adequate training and information about workplace risks

• Acting on employee concerns about safety

In Yvonne's case, the hotel had known about the slippery floor. They'd had multiple requests for non-slip footwear. They'd failed to act. That's not just bad management — it's negligence under Irish law. It's a breach of their statutory duty to keep their workers safe.

When an employer breaks this duty and a worker is injured as a result, the worker has the right to claim compensation. This is a civil claim under negligence law, separate from any health and safety enforcement action by the authorities.

What Yvonne Was Entitled To

Yvonne's compensation claim included two main parts: compensation for her pain and suffering, and compensation for her financial losses.

Pain, Suffering & Loss of Amenity

This covers the injury itself, the pain during recovery, and the ongoing discomfort. Yvonne's fractured ankle, the surgery, the physiotherapy, and the lasting ache — all of this is compensated. Irish courts use guidelines based on similar cases to set these amounts fairly.
