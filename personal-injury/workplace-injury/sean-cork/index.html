Sean's Story: Kitchen Burn and Loss of Earnings

Sean worked as a head chef in a busy Cork restaurant for eight years. He loved his job and was known for his skill and dedication. But there was a problem in the kitchen that nobody seemed to fix. One of the industrial cookers had a faulty temperature dial that didn't work properly. Sean and two other kitchen staff had reported it in writing to management three separate times over six months. Each time, they were told it would be fixed. Each time, nothing happened.

One evening during a busy service, Sean reached to adjust the cooker when it suddenly spiked to an unsafe temperature. He suffered severe burns to his right arm and hand. The injury was serious enough that he needed weeks of hospital treatment and months of physiotherapy. Worse, the nerve damage meant he couldn't grip properly anymore—something a chef needs above all else. Even after he recovered physically, the pain and limited movement meant he couldn't return to full-time chef work. He had to take a job in food management instead, earning significantly less than he had before.

Sean got legal advice and his case made clear what the restaurant had done wrong. They'd been told about the dangerous equipment multiple times in writing and had ignored it. When the injury happened, it was entirely predictable and preventable. The restaurant settled his claim, and importantly, the settlement included compensation not just for his immediate medical costs and pain, but also for his loss of future earnings as a chef. This recognised that his career prospects had changed because of an injury that never should have happened.

What the Law Says

Irish employment law requires employers to provide a safe working environment and maintain equipment properly. If an employer knows about a hazard—especially if they've been told about it in writing—they have a legal duty to fix it. If they don't, and someone gets injured as a result, the employer is liable for damages. This includes compensation for medical treatment, pain and suffering, loss of income during recovery, and importantly, loss of future earnings if the injury has affected your ability to work or earn at the same level you did before.

⏰ Important Time Limit

In Ireland, you have three years from the date of your workplace injury to take a legal claim. After three years, you lose your right to claim, with very limited exceptions. If you've been injured at work, it's important to seek legal advice as soon as possible—even if you're still recovering or unsure whether you have a case.
