When Your Employer Knew About a Danger

Thomas worked as a delivery driver for a logistics company in Kildare. His job meant spending eight hours a day on the road, moving from one delivery to the next. One morning, while driving, he noticed the van's brakes weren't responding properly. He mentioned it to his manager that same day. The manager acknowledged the fault but told him to keep using the vehicle anyway. No mechanic was called. No van was taken off the road. Thomas was simply told to manage.

Two weeks later, Thomas was making a delivery in busy traffic when the brakes failed completely. He couldn't stop in time. The van hit a parked car, and Thomas was thrown forward, suffering whiplash and a badly injured shoulder. He spent weeks in physiotherapy and months unable to work properly. The pain affected his sleep, his confidence, and his ability to do his job.

Thomas decided to take legal action. When his case was examined, it became clear that his employer had a legal responsibility to maintain safe vehicles and to remove faulty equipment from use. Documents showed that the brake problem had been reported weeks before the accident, but nothing had been done. The employer knew there was a risk and chose to ignore it. The case established negligence — the employer had breached their duty of care by knowingly allowing Thomas to drive an unsafe vehicle.

Thomas received compensation that covered his medical treatment, lost wages, and ongoing pain and suffering. More importantly, the case highlighted how seriously the law takes employer responsibility for workplace safety.

What the Law Says

Irish employment law requires employers to provide safe working conditions and safe equipment. Under the Safety, Health and Welfare at Work Act 2005, employers must assess risks, maintain equipment in good condition, and remove unsafe items from use. If an employer knows about a fault and does nothing, they are negligent. An employee injured as a result of this negligence can claim compensation for medical costs, lost income, and pain and suffering.

Important Time Limit

In Ireland, you generally have three years from the date of your injury to bring a personal injury claim. If you've had an accident at work, it's important to act quickly. Gather evidence, get medical records, and speak to a solicitor as soon as possible. Waiting too long could mean you lose your right to claim.
