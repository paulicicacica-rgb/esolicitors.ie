Hit by a Falling Object on a Neighbour's Property

Tomasz from Kildare
Renovation work — no safety barriers — full settlement reached
Tomasz was seriously injured when a piece of timber fell from scaffolding on his neighbour's property. The neighbour was having renovation work done, but no safety barriers or warning signs were in place. Tomasz pursued a claim under Irish law and reached a full settlement.

What Happened

Tomasz had lived in his home in Kildare for twelve years. His neighbour, just over the boundary, decided to undertake a major renovation project on their house. Work started on a Monday morning with scaffolding going up and contractors arriving daily. Tomasz thought little of it — these things happen in residential areas.

One afternoon in late spring, Tomasz stepped into his back garden to water his plants. The weather was clear, and he wasn't expecting any trouble. He was standing about two metres from the boundary fence when a piece of timber from the scaffolding on the neighbour's side came loose and fell directly onto his shoulder and arm. The impact was sudden and painful. He fell to the ground, his arm screaming with pain, unable to move it properly.

His wife heard his cry from inside the house and rushed out. She called an ambulance immediately. At the hospital, Tomasz was diagnosed with a fractured shoulder, a badly bruised upper arm, and soft tissue damage. He spent three nights in hospital and faced months of physiotherapy. The injury meant he couldn't work for eight weeks — he runs a small logistics business and the time away cost him considerably, both in lost income and in the work his business lost to competitors.

What made it worse was realizing that the whole accident was preventable. There had been no safety barriers erected along the boundary between his garden and the renovation site. There were no warning signs. The scaffolding was not properly secured. When Tomasz's wife looked over the fence, she could see that basic safety protocols simply hadn't been followed.

Tomasz and his wife were understandably upset. He wasn't the one doing the renovation, yet he was the one suffering the consequences. They decided to seek legal advice about their options, and that's when they discovered they had a legitimate claim under Irish property law.

What the Law Says

The Occupiers Liability Act 1995

In Ireland, property owners have a legal duty of care towards people who come onto their land. This is set out in the Occupiers Liability Act 1995. The law doesn't just apply to visitors who are invited in — it applies to anyone who has a right to be there.

The Basic Rule: A property owner must take reasonable care to ensure their premises are safe for visitors. This includes taking steps to prevent injury from hazards on their land.

When someone is doing renovation work on a property, the person who owns or controls that property is still responsible for ensuring the site is safe. This includes:

  • Erecting proper barriers and fencing
  • Displaying warning signs
  • Ensuring scaffolding and equipment are properly secured
  • Protecting neighbouring properties
