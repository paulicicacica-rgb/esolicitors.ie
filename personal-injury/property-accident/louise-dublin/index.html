How Louise Claimed Compensation After Falling Through a Rotting Step

Dublin property viewing accident claim — successful outcome under Occupiers Liability Act

Louise's Story

Louise was excited. She'd been looking for a rental property in Dublin for weeks, and this one seemed promising. A Victorian terraced house in a popular area, good price, reasonable landlord who was keen to show her around. On a Tuesday afternoon in March, she pulled up outside.

The landlord met her at the door. They walked through the downstairs rooms, discussing rent and lease terms. Then he led her towards the back door. "Garden's quite large," he said, heading out. Louise followed. The wooden back step was dark, weathered. As she placed her foot on it, the wood gave way. Her leg went straight through, and she fell hard, twisting her ankle and hitting her shin badly on the edge of the rotting timber.

The landlord helped her up, looking flustered. "I've been meaning to fix that," he said. But that was it. No first aid, no genuine concern, no documentation. Louise limped back to her car, in pain, annoyed, but not sure what to do next. She'd never made a claim before.

Over the next few days, the pain got worse. A visit to her GP confirmed she had a bad sprain and bruising. She took time off work. Physio came next—eight weeks of treatment to get her ankle strong again. The injury affected her job as a project manager; she couldn't visit building sites for two months, which meant lost commissions.

After three weeks, Louise decided to act. She contacted eSolicitors.ie, and we matched her with a solicitor who specialised in property accidents. The solicitor wrote to the landlord's buildings insurance company. Within six months, and without needing to go to court, Louise received €28,500. The insurer accepted liability immediately—the landlord had a legal duty to maintain safe premises, and a rotting step that gave way was a clear breach of that duty.

What the Law Says

The Occupiers Liability Act 1995

In Ireland, anyone who owns or controls a property—whether they live there or rent it out—has a legal responsibility to visitors. This is called "occupiers liability."

Under the Occupiers Liability Act 1995, property owners and landlords must take reasonable care to make sure their premises are safe. That means:

  • Regular inspection of the property for hazards
  • Fixing or repairing obvious problems like broken steps, loose floorboards, or damaged railings
  • Warning visitors of any dangers that do exist
  • Keeping common areas—entrances, hallways, gardens—in a safe condition

A rotting wooden step that collapses under normal use is not a minor maintenance issue. It's a serious breach. The landlord either knew about it (and did nothing) or should have known about it through regular property checks. Either way, liability was clear in Louise's case.
