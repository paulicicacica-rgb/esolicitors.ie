How Elena Won €35,000 After Tripping on Uneven Paving in a Car Park

A personal injury claim under the Occupiers Liability Act 1995
← Back to Injured on Someone Else's Property claims
Elena's story from Galway

What Happened to Elena

Elena had been visiting a private car park in Galway for work meetings for nearly three years. She knew the layout well and always parked in the same area. But on a Tuesday afternoon in March, everything changed in a split second.

As she walked from her car towards the office building, her left foot caught on a raised section of paving. The concrete had cracked and shifted, creating an uneven surface that was roughly two centimetres higher than the rest of the pathway. There was no warning tape, no sign, no barrier—nothing to tell her to watch her step. Elena went down hard, putting her hands out to try to break her fall. She fractured her wrist and badly bruised her ribs and shoulder.

What made this worse was that Elena later discovered the car park owner had received complaints about that exact spot three months earlier. A previous visitor had reported the hazard in writing, but nothing had been done. No repair. No warning. No attempt to make it safe. The owner had simply ignored it.

Elena spent two weeks off work, had physiotherapy for six weeks, and wore a support cast for eight weeks. The pain affected her sleep and her ability to do her job properly. When she finally spoke to a solicitor about what happened, she was surprised to learn she had a strong case. Under Irish law, property owners have a duty to keep their premises safe. Elena had every right to claim compensation.

What the Law Says

In Ireland, the Occupiers Liability Act 1995 sets out the legal duties that property owners and occupiers must follow. The law is clear: if you invite someone onto your property—whether it's a car park, shop, office, or restaurant—you must take reasonable care to ensure it's safe for them.

"Reasonable care" means more than just doing the bare minimum. It means the owner should:

  • Regularly inspect their property for hazards
  • Fix problems that could cause injury
  • Warn visitors of any dangers that do exist
  • Take action on complaints about safety issues

In Elena's case, the car park owner had failed all of these. The uneven paving was a known hazard (someone had reported it), it hadn't been fixed, there was no warning, and the owner had ignored the complaint. This was a clear breach of their legal duty under the Occupiers Liability Act 1995.

Because the owner was negligent, Elena was entitled to claim compensation for the injury they caused her.

What Elena's Compensation Covered

Elena received a final settlement of €35,000. This covered several different losses:

Special Damages (Out-of-Pocket Costs)

This is the money Elena actually spent because of the accident:

  • Private physiotherapy sessions: €1,200
  • Travel costs to medical appointments: €180
  • Cost of support cast and medical supplies: €95
  • Total special damages: €1,475

General Damages (Pain, Suffering, and Lost Quality of Life)

This covers the impact the injury had on Elena's life:

  • Pain and discomfort from the fractured wrist and bruising
  • Lost sleep and reduced quality of life during recovery
  • Anxiety about returning to the car park
  • Scarring and permanent sensitivity in the wrist
  • Total general damages: €11,200

Loss of Earnings

Elena lost wages while she was unable to work:

  • Two weeks completely off work: €900
  • Reduced hours for a further four weeks: €650
  • Reduced productivity over six weeks: €475
  • Total loss of earnings: €2,025

Legal Costs

The defendant's insurance company agreed to cover Elena's solicitor's fees and court costs: €20,300

Added together, Elena's total award was €35,000. This is a typical settlement for a moderate injury claim where the liability is clear and the property owner's negligence is proven.

⏰ Important: Time Limits Apply

You have 2 years from the date of your injury to make a claim.

This is the legal time limit under Irish law. Once two years have passed, you lose the right to claim compensation, no matter how strong your case is. Courts will not hear your claim if you miss this deadline.

If you've been injured on someone else's property, don't delay. Get legal advice as soon as possible. Even if you're still recovering, speaking to a solicitor now will protect your right to claim.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long do I have to make a claim?

In Ireland, you generally have three years from the date of injury to lodge a claim. However, it's best to start the process as soon as possible, as evidence is fresher and witnesses are easier to contact.

Will I have to go to court?

Most claims are settled out of court through negotiation with the property owner's insurance company. However, if a fair settlement cannot be reached, your solicitor will be prepared to take the case to court on your behalf.

What if I was partially at fault for the accident?

Ireland follows the principle of comparative negligence. Even if you were partly responsible, you may still be entitled to compensation, though the amount may be reduced based on your level of fault.

Do I have to pay upfront for legal representation?

No. Most personal injury solicitors in Ireland work on a "no win, no fee" basis, meaning you only pay if your claim is successful. Any legal fees are typically deducted from your settlement.

What damages can I claim for?

You can claim for medical expenses, loss of earnings, pain and suffering, physiotherapy costs, travel expenses related to treatment, and any other losses directly caused by your injury.
