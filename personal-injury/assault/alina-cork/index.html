Assaulted by a Colleague at Work — Alina's Story from Cork

Cork Workplace Assault Employer Negligence

What Happened

Alina worked as an office administrator for a mid-sized company in Cork city centre. She was good at her job, got on well with most colleagues, and had worked there for four years without incident. Then in March 2023, everything changed.

One afternoon, after a routine meeting, a colleague — someone she'd had minimal contact with — suddenly became aggressive. He shouted at her in front of other staff, called her names, and then physically pushed her against a desk. She fell, hit her shoulder hard, and was left shaken and in pain. The attack lasted only seconds, but it left deep marks on her arm and a lasting fear of coming to work.

What made it worse was what Alina discovered in the days that followed. She asked HR for records of complaints about this person. It turned out there had been at least three previous complaints from other employees about his aggressive behaviour. The company had done almost nothing — no formal investigation, no training, no suspension. HR had simply noted the complaints and filed them away.

Alina took sick leave, saw her GP, and was referred to a counsellor. She was diagnosed with anxiety and post-traumatic stress related to the assault. She couldn't sleep properly and felt sick every time she thought about going back to the office. Six weeks later, she decided she couldn't return to that workplace. She resigned and spent two months looking for a new job in a supportive environment.

With encouragement from a friend, Alina contacted eSolicitors.ie. She wanted to know if she could claim compensation, not just from the person who attacked her, but from the company that had failed to protect her.

How Alina Won Her Claim

The solicitor explained that Alina had two potential routes to compensation. First, she could pursue a criminal complaint — and the evidence of prior complaints made this a strong case of negligence by the employer. Second, she could make a civil claim for damages, which is separate from any criminal proceedings.

The employer's negligence was clear: they had a duty to provide a safe workplace, and they had failed. Despite knowing about this person's aggressive behaviour, they took no protective measures. That negligence made Alina's injury foreseeable and preventable. The company had breached their legal duty of care.

Alina's claim included damages for her physical injury, the psychological trauma, lost wages while she was unfit to work, and future loss of earnings (she had to accept a lower-paid job due to anxiety). Her solicitor also argued for aggravated damages — compensation for the distress caused by knowing the company had received warnings and ignored them.

The employer's insurance company reviewed the file and quickly saw they were on weak ground. They offered a settlement within three months. Rather than drag the case out, Alina accepted.

What the Law Says About Assault at Work in Ireland

In Ireland, if you are assaulted, you have legal rights — even if the person who hurt you was a colleague or an employee of the same company.

A civil claim for damages: You can sue for compensation under the law of tort, specifically for trespass to the person (assault and battery). This is separate from any criminal case.

An employer's duty: Your employer has a legal duty under the Safety, Health and Welfare at Work Act 2005 to provide a safe workplace. If they knew about a risk (like an aggressive employee) and did nothing, they have been negligent. You can claim compensation from them.

The Criminal Injuries Compensation Tribunal: If you are the victim of a violent crime in Ireland, you can apply to the Criminal Injuries Compensation Tribunal (CICT). You do not have to pursue a civil claim — it's an alternative route. The CICT can award compensation even if the attacker is never caught or convicted.

Time is important: You must bring a civil claim within two years of the assault. After that, you lose your right to compensation in most cases. The Criminal Injuries Compensation Tribunal has its own time limits (usually three years, but this can be extended if there is good reason).

What Alina Received — And What's Typical

Alina's settlement was €38,000. Here's how that breaks down across typical categories in assault cases:

  • Pain and suffering for the physical injury: €8,000. This covers the bruising, the shoulder strain, and medical treatment.
  • Psychological injury (anxiety and PTSD): €18,000. This was the largest part because the trauma was significant and ongoing. She needed counselling and was prescribed medication.
