Hit at a Junction by a Driver Who Jumped the Stop Sign

Kevin's story from Limerick: a fractured wrist, a written-off car, and a successful compensation claim

What Happened to Kevin

It was a Tuesday morning in late autumn when Kevin pulled up to a junction on the outskirts of Limerick. He'd been driving the same Ford Focus for seven years – reliable, modest, and paid for. He came to a complete stop at the red light, checked his mirrors, and waited like he had a thousand times before. That's when everything changed in the space of a second.

A silver BMW came from the left, moving fast. Too fast. The driver hadn't even attempted to brake. Kevin watched in horror as the car bore down on him, and then the impact came – a violent side-on collision that sent his Focus spinning across the junction. The sound was enormous. Glass broke. Metal crumpled. Kevin's head snapped sideways against the window frame, and a sharp pain shot through his right wrist.

The emergency services arrived within minutes. Kevin was conscious but shaken, his wrist swelling rapidly. The paramedics were professional and calm, and he was taken to the University Hospital Limerick. X-rays confirmed a fractured radius – a break that would require a plaster cast for six weeks and months of physiotherapy. His car was towed away, and the assessment was clear: write-off.

The police arrived at the hospital with news that made it worse: the other driver admitted he hadn't seen the stop sign. He'd been distracted, looking at his phone. He had insurance – at least there was that – but Kevin still felt the weight of what had happened. Time off work, pain, frustration, and the cost of replacing his car all loomed ahead. He knew he deserved answers and compensation.

What the Law Says

Under Irish road traffic law, every driver has a legal duty of care towards other road users. This means they must drive carefully and obey traffic signals – including stop signs. When a driver fails in that duty and causes an accident, they are liable for the damage and injury they cause.

In Kevin's case, the other driver had clearly breached that duty by ignoring the stop sign. The driver was insured, which meant their insurance company was responsible for compensating Kevin for his losses. Had the driver been uninsured, Kevin would have made a claim through the Motor Insurers Bureau of Ireland (MIBI), which exists to protect innocent road users in exactly these situations.

The law recognises two main types of loss in a road traffic accident claim: special damages (actual financial costs like vehicle repair, medical expenses, lost wages) and general damages (compensation for pain, suffering, and loss of enjoyment of life).

What Kevin Was Entitled To

Kevin's solicitor worked through his claim methodically. Here's what was included:

  • Vehicle damage: The write-off was valued at €9,500. Kevin was compensated in full.
  • Replacement vehicle costs: Hire car costs while his car was being assessed (€280).
  • Lost earnings: Six weeks off work at his regular salary while his wrist healed (€2,100).
  • Medical expenses: Physiotherapy sessions and additional GP visits not covered by insurance (€450).
  • General damages for injury: Compensation for the fractured wrist, pain, suffering, and the disruption to his life. Given the six-week cast and ongoing physiotherapy, this was assessed at €8,500.

In total, Kevin received €20,830 in compensation. This covered his actual losses and acknowledged the pain and inconvenience he'd suffered.

If his injury had been more severe – for example, if the fracture had required surgery or led to long-term complications – or if he'd lost significantly more time from work, the compensation could have been higher. Road traffic injury claims in Ireland typically range from €15,000 to €100,000 or more, depending on the severity of injury, age, occupation, and long-term impact on health and earnings.

⏱ Important: Know Your Time Limit

You have 2 years from the date of your accident to make a personal injury claim in Ireland. After that, your right to claim expires.

This applies even if you're still receiving treatment or haven't decided whether to claim yet. If you've been in a road traffic accident, contact a solicitor as soon as possible – don't wait.
