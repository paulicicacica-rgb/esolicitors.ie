What Happened to Seamus

Seamus was a 47-year-old father of two living in Cork City. He'd walked the same route to work for over a decade — down Parnell Street to his job at a local engineering firm. It was a journey he could do with his eyes closed. But on a Tuesday morning in October, a few seconds of carelessness by another driver changed everything.

Seamus was crossing at the pedestrian crossing near the traffic lights on Parnell Street. He was halfway across when a silver Toyota Corolla hit him head-on. The impact threw him across the bonnet and he landed hard on the road. The pain was immediate. His left leg felt broken. His ribs burned. He couldn't get up. A crowd gathered. An ambulance came within minutes.

At Cork University Hospital, the X-rays told the story: a fractured tibia, two broken ribs, a severely sprained ankle, and soft tissue damage to his back and shoulders. He spent five days in hospital. When he came home, the reality hit harder than the car had. He couldn't walk properly. He couldn't work. He couldn't even get upstairs without his wife's help. Simple tasks — making a cup of tea, showering, dressing — became agonising ordeals.

The driver who hit him claimed the lights had been amber when he entered the junction. He said Seamus stepped out unexpectedly. It was his word against Seamus's word — and the insurance company wasn't interested in settling fairly. They dragged their feet. They demanded medical reports. They suggested Seamus might have been negligent. It was stressful, exhausting, and infuriating. Seamus couldn't work. He couldn't afford a solicitor out of his own pocket. He didn't know what to do.

What the Law Says

The Road Traffic Act & Duty of Care

Under Irish law, every driver owes a legal duty of care to pedestrians and other road users. This means a driver must obey traffic lights, drive at a safe speed, watch out for pedestrians, and take reasonable steps to avoid causing injury.

When a driver violates that duty — whether by running a red light, driving recklessly, or failing to pay attention — and someone is injured as a result, the driver (and their insurance company) is legally liable for the damages.

In Seamus's case, the driver clearly breached that duty by running a red light. The CCTV footage was proof. The driver was at fault, and Seamus had every legal right to claim compensation.

What If the Driver Is Uninsured?

If a driver involved in a road traffic accident is uninsured, you can still claim compensation through the Motor Insurers Bureau of Ireland (MIBI). The MIBI is a statutory body that compensates road traffic accident victims when the at-fault driver has no insurance. The process is similar, and the compensation is the same — but claims to the MIBI can take longer.

What Seamus Was Entitled To

How Personal Injury Compensation Is Calculated

Personal injury compensation in Ireland falls into two main categories:

  • Special Damages: Concrete, quantifiable losses — medical costs, hospital bills, physiotherapy, prescription charges, lost wages, transport costs, and any equipment you needed to buy (crutches, cushions, etc.).
  • General Damages: Compensation for pain, suffering, loss of enjoyment of life, emotional distress, and any long-term impact on your quality of life.
