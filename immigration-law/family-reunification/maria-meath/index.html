Maria's Journey: Bringing Her Son to Ireland

How one Meath resident navigated DNA evidence requirements and found a path forward using documentary proof alone.

Maria became an Irish citizen after several years living and working in the country. When she decided to bring her young son from abroad to live with her, she felt hopeful about the future. But when she started the family reunification process, an immigration officer raised a concern: they wanted DNA evidence to confirm the biological relationship between Maria and her son. It wasn't a requirement Maria had anticipated, and it felt like an unexpected barrier to having her family together.

Maria was uncertain about what this meant for her application. She worried it might delay everything, and she wasn't comfortable with the idea of genetic testing for such a straightforward family matter. That's when she reached out to a solicitor who specialises in immigration and family reunification. The solicitor listened to her situation carefully and reviewed all the documents she had—birth certificates, legal guardianship papers, school records, and evidence of her ongoing relationship with her son.

Together, they built a strong case using documentary evidence alone. The solicitor explained that while immigration authorities can request DNA evidence in some cases, it isn't always necessary when a clear paper trail exists. They prepared a detailed submission showing Maria's relationship to her son through official records and correspondence. The approach worked. Maria's application progressed without the need for genetic testing, and within a reasonable timeframe, her son joined her in Meath. The process wasn't as smooth as she'd hoped at the start, but with the right legal guidance, they found a practical solution that respected both the rules and Maria's family situation.

What the Law Says

Irish family reunification law allows Irish citizens to sponsor close family members—including children—to live in Ireland. When a biological relationship needs to be proven, authorities may ask for birth certificates, court orders, or other legal documents. DNA testing can be requested in cases where documentation is unclear or missing, but it is not automatically required. Each application is assessed individually based on the evidence presented and the specific circumstances of the case.

⏱ Important: Time Limits

Family reunification applications must be started promptly. There are no fixed statutory deadlines for the overall process, but delays in submitting documents or responding to requests from immigration authorities can slow your case significantly. If you're sponsoring a child, it's important to act as soon as possible to ensure the application is lodged and progressed without unnecessary delay. Don't wait to seek legal advice once concerns are raised—early guidance can help you address potential obstacles before they become serious problems.
