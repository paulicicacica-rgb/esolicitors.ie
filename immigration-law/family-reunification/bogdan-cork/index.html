Bogdan's Path to Family Reunification

How proper documentation made the difference in bringing his wife to Cork

Bogdan had been living in Cork for three years on a Stamp 4 visa, building a stable life and establishing himself in his career. When his wife decided to leave her home country and join him in Ireland, it felt like the natural next step. They'd been apart long enough, and they were ready to start their next chapter together. Bogdan began gathering documents and submitted an application to bring her to Ireland under the family reunification scheme.

Six months later, the application came back with a refusal letter. Bogdan was devastated. The letter gave reasons, but they weren't entirely clear to him, and he didn't know what had gone wrong or how to fix it. He felt stuck—disappointed, confused, and unsure whether trying again would just lead to another rejection. At that point, he decided to seek proper legal advice.

When Bogdan met with a solicitor, they went through the refusal letter carefully. It turned out the issue wasn't with his application itself or his wife's eligibility—it was about missing documentation. The previous submission had lacked certain proof of financial stability and accommodation that the immigration authorities needed to see. The solicitor knew exactly what documents would satisfy those requirements and helped Bogdan and his wife gather everything properly. Within weeks, they had prepared a strong second application with all the necessary evidence in place.

Six months after resubmitting, Bogdan received news that his wife's application had been approved. She joined him in Cork not long after, and they began their life together properly. Bogdan has learned that immigration law can be detailed and unforgiving, but getting the right guidance at the right moment made all the difference.

What the Law Says

Family reunification in Ireland allows non-EEA nationals on a Stamp 4 visa to sponsor their spouse or children to join them, provided they can demonstrate they have adequate financial resources, suitable accommodation, and meet other legal requirements set by the Department of Enterprise, Trade and Employment. Applications must be thorough and complete—missing documents or incomplete information is the most common reason for refusal. If an application is refused, applicants have the right to seek a review or reapply with corrected documentation, though there are important time limits to be aware of.

⏰ Important Time Limits

Application Processing: Family reunification applications typically take 4–8 months to process, though this can vary.

Reapplication Windows: If your application is refused, you may reapply, but any significant delays between refusal and reapplication could affect your case if circumstances have changed.

Visa Validity: Ensure your own visa status (such as your Stamp 4) remains valid throughout the process and remains valid when your family member arrives.

Documentation Currency: Many supporting documents (bank statements, accommodation proof, employment letters) should be recent—typically dated within 3 months of submission.

Action Required: If your application has been refused or you're unsure about what documents you need, seek legal advice promptly to avoid further delays or repeated rejections.
