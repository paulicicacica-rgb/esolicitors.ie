1. Legal requirements for divorce in Ireland

Ireland introduced divorce in 1996 following a referendum. The law changed significantly through the Family Law Act 2019, which reduced the separation requirement and made divorce more accessible.

To obtain a divorce in Ireland, three conditions must be met:

  • You and your spouse must have lived apart for at least two of the previous three years
  • There must be no reasonable prospect of reconciliation
  • Proper arrangements must be in place for dependent family members, particularly children
The 2019 change

Before November 2019, couples had to be separated for four of the previous five years. The 2019 referendum reduced this to two of the previous three years, making divorce significantly more accessible.

What does "living apart" mean?

"Living apart" does not necessarily mean living in different homes. Irish courts recognise that financial constraints often mean separating couples continue under the same roof. You must demonstrate genuinely separate lives — separate finances, no shared social activities, sleeping separately. This is harder to establish legally and a solicitor's advice is important if this applies to you.

Do you need to go to court?

Not necessarily. The majority of divorces in Ireland are resolved through negotiation between solicitors and never require either party to appear before a judge. If both parties reach a consent order — a written agreement covering all main issues — the court simply ratifies it.

2. Separation vs divorce — which applies to you

Many people in Ireland confuse judicial separation and divorce. The difference is fundamental.

FeatureJudicial SeparationDivorce
Ends the marriageNoYes
Free to remarryNoYes
Waiting periodNone2 of last 3 years apart
Legal arrangements for home, children, financesYesYes
Can be converted to divorce laterYesN/A

Judicial separation is chosen when couples have not yet met the two-year requirement, when one party has religious objections to divorce, or when they want legal clarity without the finality of ending the marriage. It creates legally binding arrangements and can later be converted into a full divorce.

Divorce ends the marriage entirely and allows both parties to remarry. It is the right choice when the marriage is definitively over and both parties want a clean legal break.

3. How the divorce process works in Ireland

Step 1 — Get legal advice

The first step is always to consult a family law solicitor. Your solicitor will advise you on your rights, likely outcomes, and the best approach for your specific circumstances. Initial consultations typically cost €150–€250 and are money extremely well spent.

Step 2 — Negotiate a settlement

Your solicitor communicates with your spouse's solicitor to negotiate the terms — arrangements for children, the family home, maintenance, and financial matters. This can take weeks to several months depending on complexity and cooperation.

Step 3 — Issue court proceedings

Even in an agreed divorce, court proceedings must be formally issued. This involves filing a Family Law Civil Bill in the Circuit Court (most cases) or the High Court (high-value cases). Your solicitor handles all paperwork.

Step 4 — Consent order or court hearing

If both parties have agreed on all issues, the court is presented with a consent order and typically grants the divorce at a brief hearing. If there are disputed issues, a full hearing is required.

Step 5 — Decree of divorce

The court issues a Decree of Divorce, which legally ends the marriage.

Important

All family law proceedings in Ireland are held in camera — private, with no public or press access. What happens in the courtroom stays there.

4. What happens to the family home

The family home is almost always the most significant and emotionally charged issue in any divorce. Irish law provides strong protections.

The Family Home Protection Act 1976

Under this Act, neither spouse can sell, mortgage, or transfer the family home without the written consent of the other — even if only one name is on the deeds. If your spouse is attempting to sell without your agreement, a solicitor can take immediate steps to prevent this.

What are the options?

  • One party buys out the other — the spouse who stays pays the other their share of the equity
  • The home is sold and proceeds divided — common where neither party can afford the mortgage alone
  • Deferred sale — the home is kept until children reach a certain age, then sold and proceeds divided
If you are renting

Both names on a lease means both parties have legal rights to the property. A solicitor can advise on how to handle this cleanly and fairly under the Residential Tenancies Act.

5. Children — custody, access and guardianship

Irish family law distinguishes between three concepts that are often confused:

  • Guardianship — the right to make decisions about a child's upbringing, education, and welfare
  • Custody — where the child primarily lives and who has day-to-day care
  • Access — the right of the non-custodial parent to spend time with the child

Married fathers are automatic joint guardians. Unmarried fathers must be registered on the birth certificate (after January 2015) or apply to court. If you are an unmarried father, address this immediately with a solicitor.

The guiding principle

In every case involving children, Irish courts apply one overriding principle: the best interests of the child are paramount. In practice, this almost always means both parents remain actively involved in the child's life.

Relocation — can one parent move abroad?

A parent with custody cannot move abroad with the children without the agreement of the other parent or a court order. Attempting to do so is a serious legal matter. Get legal advice before making any decisions.

6. Finances, maintenance and pensions

Maintenance

The court can order spousal maintenance and child maintenance. There is no fixed formula in Ireland. The court looks at income and earning capacity of each party, financial needs and obligations, and the standard of living during the marriage.

Pension rights

Pensions are a matrimonial asset and must be considered in any divorce settlement. The court can issue a Pension Adjustment Order, directing the pension provider to pay a portion to the other spouse. This is technically complex — specialist advice is essential if significant pension assets are involved.

Statement of Means

In contested financial matters, both parties complete a Statement of Means — a detailed document covering income, expenditure, assets, and liabilities. Completing this accurately is critical. Understating assets or income can have serious legal consequences.

The single most effective way to reduce costs

The more you and your spouse can agree on before engaging solicitors, the lower your costs will be. Even broad agreement on children's arrangements and the family home can save thousands.

8. Legal aid — do you qualify

The Legal Aid Board provides civil legal aid for family law matters to people who cannot afford a solicitor. To qualify, your disposable income must generally be below €18,000 per year. If you qualify, you typically pay a contribution of €130 for legal advice and €130 for legal aid.

How to apply

Apply to your nearest Law Centre. Find it at legalaidboard.ie. Bring payslips, bank statements, and documentation about your situation.

10. Emotional support during divorce — why it matters

The legal process is only one dimension of divorce. The other — often the harder one — is everything it does to you as a person. The grief of a relationship ending. The fear about your children and your future. The financial uncertainty. The paperwork arriving just as you are least equipped to deal with it.
